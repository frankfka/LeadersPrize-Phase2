Atlanta sports still defined by perplexing disappointment, inevitable heartbreak

NFL
February 6, 2017 11:50am EST February 6, 2017 11:48am EST Atlanta can't have nice sports things. No matter how good things look, no matter the talent level displayed during the season, come playoff time, Atlanta teams excel at leaving expectations unmet.

Jason Foster @ByJasonFoster

Published on Feb. 6, 2017

Updated at 11:50 a.m. ET

From the moment Joe Buck informed the Fox TV audience that no Super Bowl team had ever blown a two-touchdown lead, Falcons fans knew.

They wished they didn’t know. But, if they were honest with themselves, they definitely knew.

They knew what was coming. They knew it wasn’t going to end well. Because it rarely ends well for Atlanta teams in elimination situations.

MORE: Three reasons Falcons suffered colossal collapse

See, Atlanta can't have nice sports things. Not in football, not in baseball, not in basketball, not even in the Olympics. Sunday confirmed it yet again. No matter how good things look, no matter the talent level displayed during the season, come playoff time Atlanta teams excel at leaving expectations unmet.

It's bad enough that the Falcons' epic collapse in Super Bowl 51 on Sunday night will now be the collapse by which all other collapses are measured, perhaps across all sports for the rest of time. But that it happened to Atlanta is both unsurprising and perplexing for a fanbase that's grown to expect such unhappy endings. 

"Atlanta sports fans, if they've put in the time, always expect the other shoe to drop," said Matthew Hand, 43, a Falcons fan from Cumming, Ga. "Among my friends and family, we refused to get ahead of ourselves. We weren't going to get comfortable (Sunday) unless we had a two-possession lead at the 2-minute warning. That's just how it is." 

Hand adds: "What happens to Atlanta is almost supernatural. ... I want to scream."

This is Atlanta sports. It's always something.

Well, not always always. The city, of course, does have one championship among the major sports, which is why members of the 1995 Braves can eat free anywhere in the Atlanta region in perpetuity. Still, 1995 was a long time ago. A distant memory for older fans, and not a memory at all for younger ones. In some ways, it's like it never even happened. So, in a sense, yes: always.

MORE: The Falcons' Super Bowl 51 choke, in five acts

It's easy for pleasant memories of that 1995 Braves campaign to be quickly overshadowed by so many other thoughts of what might have been. Thoughts of whatever it is that causes this to keep happening. A brief sampling of the sadness:

It's Lonnie Smith hesitating at second base in Game 7 of the 1991 World Series.

It's Mark Wohlers hanging a slider to Jim Leyritz in 1996.

It's the Falcons blowing a 17-point playoff lead to the 49ers in 2013.

It's the 60-win, top-seeded Hawks falling flat in a sweep against the Cavaliers in 2015.

It's the Falcons on Sunday in Super Bowl 51, doing the inexplicable.

Yes, the other shoe (almost) always drops.

The faces change, the sports change and the methods change, but the refrain is quite familiar: Almost had it. So close. Why can't we have nice things?

"This one takes the cake and it's going to hurt for a long time," Matt Cason, 26, of Woodstock, Ga., said of Sunday's epic Super Bowl collapse.

Still, Cason and other Atlanta fans know they don't share the previous agony of Cubs fans or, pre-2004, Red Sox fans. But they do share agony, no mistake about it.

"The agony is real, but we as fans can't automatically be fearful of heartbreak when it comes to Atlanta sports," Cason said. "... It stings. it's unsettling. But when we win a championship, It's just going to make the experience so much sweeter."

MORE: Falcons' heartbreaker isn't Kyle Shanahan's mess to clean up

Hand agrees, willing to accept the fan's version of a moral victory. 

"I refuse to give in to despair," he said. "Better to be on the cusp than in the gutter."

Some sports fans believe in curses to explain their team's repeated failures. Atlanta fans don't subscribe to any curse. They know their teams fail for actual reasons, even though at times it feels like being Atlanta-based is reason enough.

But there's also Murphy's Law, which says that whatever can go wrong will go wrong, and at the worst possible time.

One can't blame Atlanta sports fans if they're convinced this was conceived with their teams in mind.
