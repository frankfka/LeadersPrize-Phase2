Super Bowl 2019: Saints fans still bitter in face of enthusiastic Atlanta volunteers | Super Bowl 53

Updated January 28, 2019 at 9:44 AM; Posted January 28, 2019 at 9:29 AM

By Ryan Dunleavy | NJ Advance Media for NJ.com

ATLANTA — Bitter Saints fans exist between the terminal gates and baggage claim of the busiest passenger traffic airport in the world.

Atlanta native Lauren Tomasini is one of 10,000 volunteers who is volunteering time this week to be part of a Super Bowl LIII welcoming party for her city. The Super Bowl Host Committee is an annual part of every city that is awarded a Super Bowl.

As unsuspecting travelers with their head down zoomed past, Tomasini set up her enthusiastic spirit post Sunday beneath an escalator of Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport. She shouted the question of the week: Rams or Patriots?

“It’s dead even,” Tomasini said of her poll results. “We’ve had some people say, ‘Saints!’ Or ‘Falcons!’ It’s mostly smiles.”

Predictions for Super Bowl LIII: Rams or Patriots?

More than 25,000 volunteers signed up with little offered in return except keeping the “sweet” uniform. An interview process and background check followed.

Why volunteer to help out a billion-dollar business like the NFL without getting your piece of the pie? You are missing the basis of Southern Hospitality.

“You feel like you are a part of history,” volunteer Jasmine Hinnant said. “This is a once-in-a-lifetime kind of thing. People are surprised to see us.

“Only 10,000 made it, and only a few of us are here behind TSA. We’re inside. Some people are on the street outside in the cold.”

The job of a #Superbowl volunteer: Be helpful and courteous. These 2 took it to another level with some spirit in the airport, appreciation for being inside In uncharacteristically cold Atlanta. #sb53 #SuperBowlLIII pic.twitter.com/DG8KRKp5SY

— Ryan Dunleavy (@rydunleavy) January 28, 2019

Residents have seen their home transformed in the last few months, especially in the area called Atlanta Station, the city’s premier shopping destination.

“There is so much construction going on," Hinnant said. "They are really trying to make it tourist friendly. That’s the change we’ve seen the most.”

It’s a five-hour shift for most volunteers, who were scattered throughout other parts of the airport, too. Courteous and helpful was a mission fulfilled, but the enthusiasm of Hinnant and Tomasini stood out in the crowd.

“They only gave us one pom-pom, but we made the most of it it,” Tomasini said. “We shook that pom-pop until the streamers were coming out.”

When New York/New Jersey hosted Super Bowl XLVIII in 2014, there was a shortage of volunteers (20,000 was the initial number needed) to cover 80 locations, according to the New York Times. The NFL hired 1,500 temporary paid workers to fill the gap, a report said.

Again, why give up a free Sunday night?

“If they came to your city," Tomasini said, “you would do it, too.”
