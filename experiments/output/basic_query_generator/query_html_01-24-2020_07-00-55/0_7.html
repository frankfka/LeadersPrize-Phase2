N.J. already knows what Atlanta will find out: A Super Bowl isn’t worth what you think

Updated February 1, 2019 at 7:40 AM; Posted February 1, 2019 at 7:20 AM

By Jerry Izenberg | For The Star-Ledger

“The question for me is, where does it end. It’s corporate welfare at its most cavalier. Enough is enough. Have the Falcons, owner Arthur Blank and the NFL lost their mind in order to get this game?‘’

-- Georgia State Sen. Vincent Fort, when the legislature voted to waive sales tax on Super Bowl tickets.

ATLANTA — Like so many Super Bowl venues before it, this city has thumped its chest in triumph so ferociously, it is in desperate need of a collective civic CAT scan.

Yes, it seems to be overly proud to host Super Bowl 53 but as a wise man said of such situation — probably a defensive coordinator — all that glitters is not currency. Minneapolis, Minn., was the last Super Bowl venue to learn that lesson last year. Back in 2013, when it bid to host the 2018 game, it received an official bid book outlining 153 conditions it had to meet to host the game.

The latest in this selection series now indicates the league wants brand new stadiums for the site. Minnesota had one for which the states put up $343 million and the cities chipped in another $150 million to subsidize about half of the new joint’s price tag.

The local pols commissioned a study that seduced them into thinking the traveling winter carnival would produce $343 million, including $29 million in tax revenue. A number of realistic financial experts believes it would not. Victor A. Matheson, one such expert at the College of the Holy Cross in Worcester, Mass., explained it like to the New York Times:

“They always talk really good about that stuff and then they go off the rails. They can generate between $30 million and $130 million, a considerable amount, but’s only about a quarter or a tenth of what they claim.”

For the record, New Jersey state Sen. Loretta Weinberg saw this up close and personal when New Jersey and New York shared the host role for Super Bowl 48. The game was played in the Meadowlands. But visitors had a choice between staying in New Jersey or soaking up all that is available across the river in the city that never sleeps. Visitors from Seattle and Denver (the two competing teams) made the obvious choice. It did not enrich New Jersey, whose only role actually was to provide a stadium.

Food for thought: What would 300-pound NFL players order at a diner?

Nevertheless, New Jersey wiped out more than $5 million in ticket sales taxes for the NFL. Sen. Weinberg said deals like that "incentivizes participation in the bowl, but it leaves dollars in the pockets of the NFL."

She called for future hosts to wise up.

Matheson points out that the Minnesota pregame study claimed 230,000 nights of hotel occupancies would be banked. The number means little unless you deduct that number of occupancies when there is no Super Bowl at that time but there just might be convention, business travelers and tourists.

And what about the army of policemen, fireman, EMT technicians, street cleaners and snow-plow operators? Does the NFL pay the considerable overtime costs if there’s a snowstorm?

Every study makes much of the talk of the hotel revenues. But that money will not stay here any more than it did in New Jersey. That money goes to distant corporate headquarters of the hotel companies -- which means the home state rarely gets to see it, much less bank it.

But as someone who has watched all 52 of these Super Bowls, I have to admit that once the phony trappings crawl off center stage, you cannot deny America’s fascination with what it about to follow. Except for the rare blowout, there is exceptional drama.

Who can ignore what the Eagles gave us last year? Or the impossible moment when David Tyree plucked a football off the top of his helmet and broke New England’s collective heart? Or when St. Louis Rams linebacker Mike Jones and Tennessee Titans receiver named Kevin Dyson seemed to hang above the field on the 1-yard line in an airborne embrace on the last play of Super Bowl 34?

If Dyson falls forward, the Titans tie the game. If he falls backward, the Rams win.

The gasp of all the lungs in a sold-out stadium sounded like the world’s largest vacuum cleaner trying to suck up Lake Michigan. Still the pride, all the glory and all the money were there in that frozen instant. It was like their own private Super Bowl, and Jones won it.

This is the bonus we generally get.

Atlanta, however, has to pay the bills.

Star-Ledger Columnist Emeritus Jerry Izenberg is one of two daily columnists who have covered every Super Bowl.
