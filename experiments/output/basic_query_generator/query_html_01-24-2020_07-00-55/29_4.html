Portland schools systemically fail students of color, state audit says

Updated January 9, 2019 at 10:54 AM; Posted January 9, 2019 at 10:45 AM

By Eder Campuzano | The Oregonian/OregonLive and Betsy Hammond | The Oregonian/OregonLive

The Oregonian/OregonLive and Betsy Hammond | The Oregonian/OregonLive

Portland Public Schools systematically fails to provide adequate support to its schools that enroll a concentration of low-income students of color and, as a predictable result, produces poorer outcomes for those students than nearly all its peer districts in Oregon, a new state audit found.

The district doesnt take sensible steps to place excellent principals in those high-need schools and then focus on keeping them there for many years, nor has it worked to reduce high absenteeism and turnover among teachers in those same schools, the year-long review by the Secretary of States Audit Division found. Students and families of color who would benefit greatly from trusting, lasting relationships with highly skilled educators instead encounter a revolving door of principals and new-to-the-profession teachers, it said.

The audit compared Portlands results including test scores, how many classes freshmen pass, graduation rates and college-going rates to those in the 12 other largest districts in Oregon plus two smaller districts in or near Portland. When it comes to low-income Latino students, 11 of those districts clearly outperformed Portland Public Schools, the audit found. Of the nine districts with enough low-income black students to make a valid comparison, five clearly outperformed Portland and none did worse.

The district tends to overload high-needs schools with too many new initiatives, the audit found. And the district has not carried through on plans to redraw attendance boundaries so that high-poverty high-minority schools such as Martin Luther King Jr. School, with just 320 students across six grades, have enough students to offer robust programming.

The district has only this year begun to develop a uniform core curriculum for all schools and to improve and standardize instructional training for teachers and principals, the audit found. After wholesale change in leadership, the district has made improvements and plans more, but it risks taking too long to complete needed changes, auditors wrote.

In a national comparison, some districts with less money per pupil to spend than Portland, including Charlotte, Austin and the Cherry Creek district near Denver, nevertheless achieved superior results on key metrics, the audit found. Portland spends at least 20 percent more per student than the 12 next largest districts in Oregon, the audit found. Most of those serve a higher share of impoverished students than Portland does.

Portlands performance evaluations for teachers and principals conducted in 2016-17 were generally short on specifics and suggestions for improvement and too tolerant of mediocre performance, the audit said. The district hasnt conducted a performance audit in three years, a spokesman conceded. But it is on the verge of hiring two new auditors to restart that function, district officials said.

Oregons largest school district is on an upward trajectory, the audit found. It recently created two middle schools to better serve more than 1,000 middle-school-aged students who would otherwise have attended anemic K-8 schools, and it is working diligently to establish a uniform core curriculum, it said. Its performance with middle-class white students is quite strong, compared with peer districts in Oregon and around the country.

This school year, it has more than doubled how much coaching and supervision it provides to principals and also instituted uniform interim testing in reading and math to help teachers adjust instruction to improve year-end results, according to feedback from district officials included in the audit document. It also hired a firm to help provide training and support to help schools institute a tiered system for encouraging good behavior and school culture and responding effectively when students misbehave.

Still, more decisive action is needed, auditors said, to better serve the students who most depend on public schools to give them a shot at climbing the economic ladder: low-income students, particular those of color.

Portland Superintendent Guadalupe Guerrero said he agrees its important to provide extra training, support, encouragement, appreciation and pay to principals and teams of strong teachers at high-needs schools so they will stick around.

This is going to take some work. But its work weve already embarked on, he told reporters Tuesday afternoon.

Guerrero did not specify any steps the district has taken or will take to make that happen, but said he had begun the conversation with the teachers union.

Leaders of that union refused to speak with state auditors.

School board member Julia Brim-Edwards and board chair Rita Moore both took issue with the states audit, contending it gives an outdated picture of their district, which they agree was deeply dysfunctional and adrift before they, fellow new board members and Guerrero came on board. They emphasized strides they have made, including getting Guerrero and a new leadership team in place, righting the districts overly costly and litigious legal portfolio, increasing transparency and fixing health and safety threats.

Its been a heavy lift and weve gotten the district back on track in several areas, Brim-Edwards said.

Republicans in the state Legislature immediately cited the audit as a reason not to pump $2 billion more into Oregon schools, as Gov. Kate Brown has said to the applause of school advocates that she aims to see happen. They cited Portlands large racial achievement gap and pointed to misspending, such as a June 2017 $13,000 retirement party held on the Portland Spirit tour ship featuring expensive crystal gifts and leis flown in from Hawaii.

That was exactly the sort of since-discontinued practice Brim-Edwards and Moore said they feared would be highlighted.

The audit faulted state leaders for failing to adequately support Portland and other districts.

The Oregon Department of Education hasnt effectively helped them turn around their struggling schools or carry out effective improvement plans because it doesnt have much authority and doesnt make efficient use of the power it does have, the audit said. Half of the schools that the department got special federal funding to assist either didnt improve their results or actually got worse on the states watch, auditors found.

The departments regulations lack clarity and enforceability, auditors wrote. Its monitoring system is basically a checklist that districts themselves fill out, it said. That increases the risk that state-level education improvement strategies -- most notably, strategies focused on struggling students -- will not take hold, the audit says.

The state should be much more transparent about its efforts to help improve the states lowest performing schools, the auditors said. Whether and how much those schools improve should be published as part of the states annual report card on school performance, they said. And the state should evaluate -- and tell the public -- every year which of the federally funded interventions it is using with low-performing schools are working and which are not.

The state should also do a better job of monitoring large grants it awards to improve results for students, the audit said. It should set specific goals, including numeric metrics, and should follow through and measure whether grantees are meeting them.

Colt Gill, Oregons state schools chief, wrote that he and his department agree with auditors finding and recommendations. Some fixes were already underway, he said.

The audit, he wrote, reinforces the pathway that (the department) began more than two years ago to tighten financial oversight of taxpayer funds, streamline its federal grants and programs and shift from a shame and blame approach towards schools to a results-driven, equity- and partnership-based plan for serving all Oregon students.

-- Eder Campuzano and Betsy Hammond
