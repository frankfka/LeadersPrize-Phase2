Ohio Payday lending bill: Who wins and who loses?

By Laura Hancock, cleveland.com | Posted July 12, 2018 at 12:05 PM | Updated July 12, 2018 at 12:13 PM
Comment
(Nati Harnik/Associated Press)

Dozens of people both in and outside Ohio -- worked hard for and against a bill restricting payday lending, which passed the Ohio Senate Tuesday. 

House Bill 123 is poised to become law, although it still needs a final signoff from the Ohio House, which isn't meeting again until at least September. Then it would head to Gov. John Kasich for his signature.

The bill limits payday loans interest rates to 28 percent. Interest rates have no limit today because payday lenders skirted interest rate requirements from 2008 and register under a part of Ohio law that doesnt cap interest or fees.

The Pew Charitable Trusts says Ohio's average interest rate is the nations highest at 591 percent, a figure the industry disputes. 

So who wins and who loses under the new restrictions? Read on for a list.



Winners: People who need short-term loans


(Elise Amendola, Associated Press file)

Payday lenders in Ohio say theyve served 1 million people in the state. 

Research shows many are low- and middle-income people who have poor credit or lack bank accounts. 

H.B. 123 could save these people $75 million a year. 

Under the Senate's version of H.B. 123, which it calls the Fairness in Lending Act:

  • The maximum loan limit would be $1,000.
  • Terms of the loan would last no more than 12 months. 
  • The cost of the loan - fees and interest - cannot exceed 60 percent of the loan's original principal. 
  • The interest rate would be no more than 28 percent, aligning with what voters upheld at the polls in 2008.
  • There would be no loans under 90 days unless the monthly payment is not more than 7 percent of a borrower's monthly net income or 6 percent of gross income. 
  • Borrowers would be prohibited from carrying more than a $2,500 outstanding principal across several loans. Payday lenders would have to make their best effort to check their commonly available data to figure out where else people might have loans. The substitute bill also authorizes the state to create a database for lenders to consult.
  • Lenders could charge a monthly maintenance fee that's the lesser of 10 percent of the loan's principal or $30.
  • For loans that last longer than 90 days, the lenders would have to provide the consumers with a sample repayment schedule based on affordability.
  • Harassing phone calls from lenders would be prohibited.
  • The lender would have to provide loan cost information orally and in writing.
  • Borrowers would get 72 hours to change their minds about the loans and return the money, without paying any fees. 

This is assuming the payday lenders and their attorneys don't find a loophole to skirt H.B. 123. The reason the bill is in the legislature in the first place is because they found a loophole to get around a previous law.
