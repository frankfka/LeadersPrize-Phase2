OpenSecrets.org Mailbag: Digging into Federal Guidelines, Filing Deadlines and more

by Dan Hartranft on May 3, 2012

Welcome to another OpenSecrets.org Mailbag. This week’s questions deal with the difference between a small individual contribution and a large individual contribution, as well as the FEC’s filing deadlines.

====

QUESTION: Hi, I was visiting your site, and saw a breakdown of “Small Individual Contributions.”  I could not find where “small” was defined.  Is it less than $500? –Bill Hensley

ANSWER: Great question, Bill. When we refer to “small” donations — or “unitemized” ones, in campaign finance parlance — we are referring to contributions of $200 or less to federal candidates, PACs or party committees.

Here’s why that number matters: under the Federal Election Commission’s rules, campaigns aren’t required to register personal information about any donor of less than $200. If a contribution is $200 or more, however, then it is considered a large, or “itemized” donation, and the campaign receiving the contribution must disclose the donor’s name, occupation, employer and ZIP code.

But don’t think you can make lots of contributions of $200 or less without disclosure. Multiple donations that total more than $200 from a single person must be reported, just as large contributions are. A person could write five $100 checks to a campaign, with each one of those contributions technically considered as small. However, that person has given $500 to the campaign, and the FEC requires the campaign to disclose the donor’s information.

 

Our research has shown that large-dollar donations account for the overwhelming majority of contributions in U.S. elections. According to the Donor Demographic section of OpenSecrets.org, such contributions made up nearly 68 percent of individual donations to candidates, PACs, and parties in 2010. OpenSecrets.org also provides a breakdown of the sources of funds for individual candidates. President Obama, for example, relies on large money contributions for 56 percent of his total fundraising.

For other definitions, you can use our glossary. The FEC’s guide for candidates provides a much more technical explanation of small and large dollar donations.

====

QUESTION: Within the last three or four months, I made several campaign contributions of varying amounts to several candidates (representing both sides of the aisle) at the state and federal level, but I cannot locate any of these on your  website. Can you please tell me how to locate these contributions?  – Anonymous

ANSWER: We only track federal-level political donations here at OpenSecrets.org, so the website won’t show any contributions to state or local candidates. Also, if you make a contribution of $200 or less to a federal candidate, the campaign is not required to disclose your identity to the FEC (as we learned above). We calculate that donation as part of a aggregate “unitemized” figure.

Also, the FEC’s filing deadlines differ for federal candidates and party organizations, which tends to create a lot of confusion. Presidential campaign committees, party organizations, and many political action committees file on a monthly basis. The requirements for super PACs vary; some report monthly while others report twice a year.

U.S. House and Senate candidates, on the other hand, only have to file on a quarterly basis. If you gave to a candidate for U.S. House or Senate in the time period between January 1 and March 31, it fell in the first quarter of the year. The first quarter filing deadline for campaigns is April 15. Once the campaigns send their donor information to the FEC, it still takes a little time — but not much! — for the research team here at the Center for Responsive Politics to process the data and update the website.

But the question remains: How would you find a federal-level donation on OpenSecrets.org were it to meet FEC disclosure requirements? Simple: Use our Donor Lookup tool.

Donor Lookup allows users to look up contributors giving large-dollar donations to federal candidates, PACs, party committees. You can use different criteria to fuel a search, from the donor’s name to her employer or even ZIP code.

You can also find information about state-level donations at FollowtheMoney.org, the website run by the National Institute for Money in State Politics.

Center for Responsive Politics Senior Research Douglas Weber assisted in providing these answers.
