Tax cut for job creators advances

Incentives for relocated businesses also moving ahead

Jan. 25, 2011
0 Comments
SHARE

By Jason Stein of the Journal Sentinel

Jan. 25, 2011 0

Madison â The Assembly Tuesday approved Gov. Scott Walker's tax cut bill for businesses, and the Senate sent the Republican governor two more tax incentive measures ahead of his "state of the state" speech next week.

A key part of Walker's special legislative session on job creation, the tax cut bill was scaled back by the governor from a campaign proposal for small businesses and then went through further changes by Republican lawmakers before its passage.

The other two bills given final passage by the Senate in bipartisan votes Tuesday included a measure to add $25 million in tax credits to an economic development program and another to wipe away the taxes for two years of businesses moving to the state.

The tax cut bill passed the Assembly 60-33, with three Democrats and one independent joining all the Republicans to support it. It now goes to the Senate, where Republicans are expected to pass it on Thursday.

"This is a massive attitude adjustment for the state of Wisconsin," said Rep. Mark Honadel (R-South Milwaukee), adding it sends a message to businesses that state officials are focusing on them.

Under the bill, businesses with sales of up to $5 million annually would receive a tax deduction of $4,000 for each job they create. Businesses with sales of more than $5 million a year would receive a deduction of $2,000 for each job they create.

Overall, the proposal would save businesses an estimated $33.5 million in taxes and cost the state that amount in lost revenue. The actual state tax savings per job would amount to between $92 and $316, depending on how the business owner files and what tax rate applies to him or her, according to an analysis by the nonpartisan Legislative Fiscal Bureau.

Democrats said that wouldn't be enough to persuade a business to add employees, with Rep. Mark Pocan (D-Madison) comparing the bill to an expensive "fast food" meal.

"We're putting out bills very, very quickly, but they're doing very little on jobs and the economy," Pocan said.

With the state facing an up to $3.3 billion shortfall in the 2011-'13 budget, passing the proposal would mean lawmakers and Walker would have to find more spending cuts later. Overall, the bills in Walker's special session would increase the 2011-'13 shortfall by up to $142 million, including the estimated $49 million added to the next budget's deficit when Walker on Monday signed a bill giving state tax breaks for health savings accounts.

Democrats raised concerns about the bill's effects on the state's finances, particularly when Walker announced this week the state faces a separate shortfall in the fiscal year that ends June 30. He said the Legislature would have to take up a budget repair bill, but has not said how big the budget gap is.

Walker has said the proposals will help him keep his promise of creating 250,000 jobs. Walker will be able to tout them as he addresses lawmakers and the rest of the state next week in his first "state of the state" address.

Also Tuesday:

â¢ The Assembly passed 57-36 a bill that would require a two-thirds vote in both houses of the Legislature to raise the state sales tax or income tax. The bill is largely symbolic because a law passed by one Legislature cannot bind what lawmakers do in the future, although Republicans are also considering a constitutional change that would be more durable. The measure now goes to the Senate.

â¢ The Senate approved 24-9 a bill that would provide a total personal income or corporate tax break for two years for businesses that relocate here. The incentive would be retroactive to Jan. 1 and would lower state revenues by an estimated $1 million over two years. Firms receiving it cannot have done business in the state for at least two years before moving here. It now goes to Walker.

â¢ The Senate voted 25-8 to add $25 million to the Economic Development Tax Credit program, bringing it to $98.1 million. The credit can go to businesses that locate or retain corporate headquarters in Wisconsin, create jobs, make capital investments or train workers. The bill now goes to Walker.

The proposed tax cut for businesses approved by the Assembly has gone through several different forms. On the campaign trail, Walker said he would shave 1 percentage point off the income taxes of businesses with 50 or fewer employees. But after he was elected, he replaced that idea with a plan to give a tax break to businesses with $500,000 or less in gross sales.

That idea would have cost the state $40 million a year in revenue - $6.5 million more than the version the Assembly ultimately passed.

The size of the tax break would depend on a business owner's or company's tax rate. For instance, the state corporate tax rate is 7.9% and the state individual income tax rate varies from 4.6% to 7.75%, depending on the taxpayer's income.

Patrick Marley of the Journal Sentinel staff contributed to this article.
