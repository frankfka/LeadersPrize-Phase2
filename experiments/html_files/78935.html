Walker calls for more privatization in 'state of the county' speech

By Steve Schultze of the Journal Sentinel
Jan. 12, 2010

Milwaukee County Executive Scott Walker kept his foot to the floor on outsourcing as a solution to the county's financial woes, in his annual "state of the county" speech Tuesday.

Walker called for privatizing courthouse security, airport operations and park maintenance - all ideas that he's broached before but that were rejected by the County Board. With the lingering recession and more pressing fiscal problems, Walker said he hoped the privatization plans get a better reception this year.

"We will continue to streamline county government by contracting out for services, consolidating departments and enhancing the use of technology," he said.

Walker also said he would propose a property tax levy reduction for 2011.

"Simply freezing taxes at current levels will not be enough to turn our economy around," Walker said at TAPCO, a Brown Deer firm.

Walker's eight budgets have each proposed tax levies at the same level approved the prior year. He has described that as a freeze, while critics note he regularly has embraced increases approved by the County Board for his budgets for the following years.

In the fall Walker proposed a $257.6 million levy for 2010, up $38 million from his first budget in 2003.

Walker did not say how much he'll propose cutting the levy in his next budget, which comes out in September. That puts it in the midst of the 2010 race for governor. Walker is a Republican candidate for that office.

His support for additional privatizations follows a long-term effort in that area, with mixed success. The county executive called for outsourcing courthouse security in his 2010 budget, but that was rejected by the County Board. Walker succeeded in winning privatization of county janitorial services this year, a move Walker said would save $2.6 million.

Airport proposal revisited

In the speech, Walker said outsourcing makes economic sense, particularly in tough times. While short on detail, Walker said privatizing airport operations could enable the county to "actually turn a profit and use the revenue to pay down debt." He also said the money from an airport privatization could be used to help pay for the county's bus system without increasing the property tax.

Walker is opposed to a half-cent increase in the county sales tax for transit, a route favored by a majority of the County Board.

He sought but failed to win County Board support for a study on airport privatization for 2009, and he didn't address the issue in his 2010 budget. He has said the county could collect $500 million or more for issuing a long-term lease for airport operations to a private firm.

In an interview, Walker said he would propose hiring a consultant this year to advise the county on privatizing the airport.

Three county supervisors attending Walker's speech said they remained opposed. Supervisors Johnny Thomas, Christopher Larson and Theo Lipscomb said it would be unwise for the county to entrust one of its prime assets to private firms. They warned that fees for a variety of services at the airport would likely be increased.

Supervisor Joe Sanfelippo backs airport outsourcing. He said winning board assent would be difficult, but the tough economic times might help sway some skeptics.

Walker claimed credit for sparking a debate about reorganizing county government. Change is necessary and no cost-saving idea should be rejected out of hand, he said.

His previous comments on blowing up county government have been more pointed. But Walker said he'd still prefer to see all the services now provided by the county turned over to the state and municipalities. He also has backed creation of new special districts, perhaps for parks and transit, if overall taxes don't rise.

Much of Walker's speech focused on accomplishments of his tenure as county executive, a job he first won in a 2002 special election after the county pension scandal. He listed as successes a 20% reduction in the county workforce; issuance of pension bonds; placement of the House of Correction under Sheriff David A. Clarke Jr.; and a national award for park management.

Concessions cut deficit

Walker gave one specific nod to the County Board, saying he's pleased it partly went along with his demand for wage and benefit concessions for county employees. The 2010 budget freezes pay, imposes unpaid furlough days, limits overtime and shifts some benefit costs to employees. While those changes are in force for the 15% of the county's workers who are nonunion, the concessions still must be bargained with the eight county unions.

Walker claimed credit for cutting the county's structural deficit by $50 million this year. That figure assumes the concessions will be adopted for all union workers.

The county executive spoke in front of a blowup of a check to county taxpayers for $200 million - representing four years' worth of savings from concessions.

Milwaukee Mayor Tom Barrett, who attended Walker's speech, said he would have liked Walker to say more about job creation, which Barrett said he'll emphasize in his Democratic campaign for governor. Barrett is expected to schedule his "state of the city" address for sometime in February.

Lee Bergquist of the Journal Sentinel staff contributed to this report.
