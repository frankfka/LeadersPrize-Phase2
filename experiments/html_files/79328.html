Oppressive debt forces governments - and West Bend schools - to make tough choices

Michael Sears

Kelly Egan asks Jack Steele, 7, (right) to remember a picture in his mind as she reads a portion of a story. Egan was teaching a lesson on visualization to her first-graders at McLane Elementary School in West Bend. Her job barely survived budget cuts last year and the position may disappear next year.

By John Schmid of the Journal Sentinel

Oct. 16, 2010 |(122) Comments

Photo Gallery
Associated Press file photo
The National Debt Clock in Manhattan only ticks higher, adding pressure to government finances.

More photos

West Bend — After living beyond its means for decades and shifting its debt onto future generations, an entire society is seeing the bills come due earlier than expected. And Kelly Egan's students are about to pay the price.

Egan teaches high achievers in math and reading, a job that barely survived budget cuts last year - but the reprieve was short-lived. At the end of this school year, the position is almost certain to disappear along with dozens more in West Bend, adding to the hundreds of thousands of public employees nationwide whose employment has been cut short by the meanest economic downturn since the 1930s.

"Parents ask, 'What should we do with our children as the West Bend School District continues to cut and cut and cut programs,' " said Egan, a 20-year veteran who is likely to be reassigned to teach the regular curriculum.

For the first time since the Depression, virtually every strata of American government is caught in the same viselike squeeze: Cities, counties and states find themselves deep in debt and lacking rainy day reserves to tide them over in hard times. Even with federal stimulus funds, local governments are laying off police officers and teachers, closing firehouses and selling public assets. During the past two years, state and local governments nationwide have cut 242,000 jobs, and public schools have shed an additional 200,700, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics.

In Wisconsin alone:

• An unprecedented three of every four school districts cut teachers this year, with an estimated 1,000 educators terminated and at least as many phased out through attrition or reduced to part time, according to the Wisconsin Education Association Council.

• Pewaukee, facing a $1.8 million deficit, disbanded its Police Department on Dec. 31. The city contracted with the Waukesha County sheriff for a third fewer officers. In the Milwaukee Police Department, nearly 100 sworn positions including beat cops and detectives are going unfilled, even after the city received enough stimulus funds separately to hire and train 50 new officers for three years.

• Milwaukee County's government is in such dire financial shape that the Greater Milwaukee Committee has concluded that state lawmakers should allow it and other local governments to file for bankruptcy. The committee's draft report recommends eliminating the county executive's office, slicing benefits and spinning off the bus system, zoo and parks under separate commissions.

• According to an analysis by the Public Policy Forum, a Milwaukee think tank, the bus system for Milwaukee County is relying on stimulus funds to postpone a full-blown crisis that could force a 30% reduction in service, cutting off transit links to the suburbs and triggering a downward spiral as riders flee, fare revenue shrinks further and yet another round of cuts becomes unavoidable.

But nowhere is the squeeze more evident than in West Bend, home to the state's 20th-largest school district as well as one of its highest unemployment rates and a fervent anti-tax sentiment that makes budget-cutting the only apparent option.

Some West Bend students go to school each day in converted trailers that act as makeshift classrooms to alleviate overcrowding. Kindergarten classrooms average 25 pupils per teacher, five more than the recommended level. In middle school, the ratio is sometimes 30 or more to one.

Yet the district cut the equivalent of 18 full-time jobs this school year - a pay freeze and federal stimulus funds kept the layoffs from being worse - and Superintendent Patricia Herdrich is preparing to slash as many as 39 more to deal with a $3.5 million shortfall next year.

She also is prepared to merge the district's two high schools, a move she resisted for as long as possible. West Bend East and West Bend West already share a campus and combine many courses, administration and gyms. But a single mega-high school - with 2,400 students, it would match Kenosha's Tremper High as the state's largest - would have teams and programs so large that they would exclude all but the strongest and most talented.

"These kids are going to inherit our debt while we're cutting their programming," Herdrich said. "Meanwhile, overseas, Singapore, China and India are prioritizing education as their economic driver."

Fiscal illusions

The recession may have been the last straw, but the origins of the economic problems facing the nation's state and local governments stretch back many years.

"In the past we just borrowed more and went on our merry way," said Charles McMillion, head of the MBG Information Services forecasting firm in Washington, D.C. "The consequences of 30 years of living beyond our means are catching up with us at every level."

During the past 25 years, the number of authorized Wisconsin state government employees rose from 27,000 to 33,000. Growth peaked at 35,533 in 2002 and has since leveled off, according to an analysis of data covering all state agencies but excluding jobs in the courts, Legislature and the University of Wisconsin System.

A Republican administration under Gov. Tommy Thompson more than doubled spending from 1987 to 2001, while the number of authorized state government jobs rose 25%. Hiring rose further and then tapered off while spending was static under Gov. Scott McCallum, who completed Thompson's last term when Thompson took a cabinet post in Washington. Under Gov. Jim Doyle, a Democrat who took office in January 2003, spending rose 15% through 2009. Doyle inherited a $3.2 billion deficit that now stands at $2.7 billion. The state has left some 3,600 authorized positions vacant this year.

As deficits grew in recent years, many states, including Wisconsin, tried accounting games to deal with them. Wisconsin, for instance, reallocated $1.3 billion from a fund meant for roads, bridges and bus systems to plug budget holes. Another Wisconsin trick, a 2007 raid of a compensation fund for injured patients, was recently ruled unconstitutional.

"You can find gimmicks and fiscal illusion in almost every state," said Eileen Norcross, a senior research fellow at the Mercatus Center, a think tank associated with George Mason University, who did a study on "Fiscal Evasion in State Budgeting."

"Now the illusion is going away," Norcross said.

Wisconsin's finances look far worse when they are subjected to generally accepted accounting principles, or GAAP, the standard in the private sector. According to the Wisconsin Taxpayers Alliance, a Madison-based nonprofit watchdog group, Wisconsin was running the worst per-capita deficits in the nation even before the economy collapsed: In 2007, Wisconsin's GAAP deficit amounted to $445 per person, the worst per-capita shortfall in the nation. In 2008, the most recent year for which figures were available, Wisconsin's per-capita deficit was second-worst, behind only Illinois and just ahead of California.

"It's important for voters to understand that they can't just vote the bums out of office and it'll get better," said Anneliese Dickman, fiscal policy researcher at the Public Policy Forum.

States typically rely on Washington for about 40% of their budget, while local governments, in turn, often rely on state transfers for at least a third of their funds. Meantime, America's households are in no mood to foot higher tax bills: Household debt hovers near historic peaks reached in 2008, according to Federal Reserve Board data. "You're observing the same behavior among consumers as you do among governments," Norcross said.

It's now unclear how much longer Washington can keep borrowing. Already, the U.S. Treasury owes $846 billion to China, the nation's single biggest foreign financier.

According to an unusually blunt report from the Congressional Budget Office in July, Washington could face a renewed fiscal crisis, even as the nation has barely begun to recover from the recession.

"The exact point at which such a crisis might occur for the United States is unknown," the agency said, "in part because the ratio of federal debt to GDP is climbing into unfamiliar territory."

Taxes vs. budgets

As Wisconsin lawmakers have scrambled to plug their deficits, school districts have suffered, and little wonder: K-12 education accounts for 38% of state spending, making education a necessary part of any and all budget discussions.

For the first time on record, the state last year reduced school spending, a move that in West Bend means the district now shoulders the largest share of school funding locally. Previously, the state had paid the largest share.

At the same time, West Bend is a proudly conservative community. Tea party activists staged a major rally there in August, and there is little appetite for tax increases to offset the state cuts.

District voters approved a 2009 referendum measure to rebuild the Badger Middle School, but two others in the past four years were rejected - one would have replaced 116-year-old Jackson Elementary, among other improvements, and another would have replaced Barton Elementary, built in 1923. Barton is heated by a pair of giant steel boilers from the 1950s. "If one fails, it will plunge the school into financial crisis," said Assistant Superintendent Ted Neitzke.

"We are caught in a crossfire right now of tax relief and imploding state budgets," Superintendent Herdrich said. "The divide is ripping this community apart, literally."

Last year, when the state tightened its budget, some parents stood up at the annual school district meeting and argued for a  zero property tax increase.

"We don't have the public coming to us and saying 'good job,' " Herdrich said, emphasizing at the same time that she is not advocating higher taxes.

Still, residents are proud of high-performance schools that run on a low-tax shoestring, said John Capelle, West Bend's director of economic development. "We've had a ton of people move to West Bend in part because of the schools."

According to Robert W. Baird & Co., the district's financial adviser, West Bend nearly leads the state in frugality. Of Wisconsin's 242 districts, it boasts the 27th-lowest local tax rate for public education, even though it educates far more students (7,100) than the 26 other districts that have lower tax rates, said Baird analyst Brian Brewer. Its double-A bond rating from Standard & Poor's lands it in the state's top 15% in terms of financial management. And it ranks fourth-lowest statewide in per-pupil spending.

So with West Bend already operating on fumes, and with little hope for help from higher levels of government, Herdrich sounds grim about the future.

"Will it turn around?" she asks. "No. We're headed into a bigger state deficit. The stimulus dollars are going away. And the policy issues are not being tackled. Eventually every district will get hit, and by extension every student, and we're just on the front wave in West Bend."

Past the tipping point

The squeeze that has hit West Bend schools also is being felt around the country. In its September unemployment report, the U.S. Labor Department found that local governments eliminated jobs at the fastest rate in nearly 30 years, many of them in education. Local governments cut 76,000 jobs, and states cut an additional 7,000.

"This is just the first phase of cuts that are coming," predicts David M. Walker, the president and chief executive of the Peter G. Peterson Foundation, a government budget watchdog group in Washington, D.C.

"There will be much more belt-tightening that has to take place at the state and local level in the future," Walker said. "A vast majority of the states, over 40 of them, have serious structural deficit problems."

Until he helped launch the foundation in 2008, Walker worked for 10 years as U.S. comptroller general and head of the Government Accountability Office. For much of the past decade, he has warned that America has become a superpower living on credit, increasingly putting itself at the mercy of foreign lender nations such as China.

What has changed, he argued in an interview, is that the nation is now past the tipping point. "I and others have noted for years that all three levels of government faced structural deficit," Walker said. "These used to be off on the horizon, but they are now staring us in the face."

Walker expects state and local authorities will feel the most immediate squeeze because they lack Washington's ability to print money.

Schools have even fewer options, because they cannot furlough teachers temporarily, and they cannot charge for liquor licenses or issue parking tickets.

"This is definitely the worst economic situation that school districts have faced since the Depression," said Dan Domenech, executive director of the American Association of School Administrators.

The consequences are upon us. Three years ago, six librarians served West Bend's six elementary schools; this year, one librarian remains.

Next year, there likely will be none.
