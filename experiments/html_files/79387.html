Chancellor says UWM should add 2 campuses to boost area research

By MEGAN TWOHEY
Jan. 26, 2007

University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee Chancellor Carlos Santiago announced Thursday that he wants to establish two new campuses for the school to ease the cramped corridors of its east side campus and strengthen the region's research infrastructure.

Santiago, who has made expansion of UWM's research his top priority, envisions one campus at or near the Milwaukee Regional Medical Center and the Milwaukee County Research Park in Wauwatosa. He wants this to be the home of an engineering school and of biomedical and advanced automation research and teaching.

Another campus would be in or near a hospital or other health care provider in downtown Milwaukee. It would be the site for a public health school, and programs in nursing, psychology and other health disciplines.

The two campuses would mark the first major expansion of the university since the late 1970s.

Santiago rolled out his ambitious vision during his spring semester plenary address, a university equivalent of the president's State of the Union speech.

He said he had begun talking to private donors about the two campuses, although he would not say which ones.

"This is our way of making the announcement," Santiago said in an interview after his address.

"We are getting the word out there that we are receptive to moving parts of our university off campus."

302 students an acre

UWM is the densest campus in the UW System, a collection of 13 universities and 13 two-year colleges.

UWM serves more than 28,000 students on 93 acres, a ratio of about 302 students an acre, Santiago said. UW-Madison, by contrast, serves 41,000 students on 933 acres, or 44 students an acre.

As a result, UWM suffers from a shortage of classrooms, dorms and parking.

"We are too many people, doing too much, in too little space," Santiago said.

Some of the university's activities take place at other locations. A downtown campus houses the School of Continuing Education. The Great Lakes WATER Institute conducts research near the south side.

But these sites, Santiago said, have not helped to "decompress our east side campus."

He said it wasn't just the university that would benefit from an expansion; the region needs a stronger research infrastructure that will spur start-ups and generate highly qualified workers for the global economy.

Universities in the area, he said, generate $160 million in external research - an amount that he called "simply insufficient." The University of Illinois at Chicago, by contrast, contributes $300 million in research expenditures alone to Chicago.

Santiago said he saw the Milwaukee Regional Medical Center and the Milwaukee County Research Center as a "strategic location" where UWM could help create "a collaborative research powerhouse."

The medical center includes the Blood Research Institute, Curative Rehabilitation Services, Children's Hospital of Wisconsin, Froedtert Hospital and the Medical College of Wisconsin. UnitedHealthcare and Alterra Health Care Corp. are among the companies in the Milwaukee County Research Park. GE Healthcare sits nearby.

Good reception

Richard Katschke, associate vice president of public affairs at the Medical College, said he was unaware of UWM's plans to set up a campus nearby but liked the idea.

"This would be of great interest to the Medical College," he said. "We're always looking to do more collaboration with UW-Milwaukee. Combined efforts could lead to economic growth."

Santiago said a downtown campus with patients was needed for faculty and students in nursing and other health sciences to conduct first-rate clinical training and research.

"UW-Milwaukee cannot serve the students of this region, create tomorrow's work force and be a catalyst for growth if we continue to work almost exclusively from our east side campus," he said.

"For UWM to fully realize its mission, we must dramatically expand our intellectual space: our geographic footprints in other parts of this region."
