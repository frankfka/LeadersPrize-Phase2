Deal touts U.S. House move to repeal health care legislation

  • Print
  • E-mail

By Aaron Gould Sheinin

The Atlanta Journal-Constitution

With the U.S. House poised to vote to repeal federal health care legislation, Gov. Nathan Deal said Wednesday morning that it's a necessary move.

Deal, speaking at a Georgia Healthcare Association conference at a convention hotel near Cobb Galleria, said the federal legislation supported by President Barack Obama will be too costly to states.

"Sometimes people say, ‘Well you don’t need to prepare for the federal impact until you know whether or not it’s going to be sustained,' " Deal said of the new federal policies. "Unfortunately, that is not the luxury we have."

Deal said the legislation, adopted last year while he was still a member of Congress, would add 650,000 Georgians to the state's Medicaid rolls at a cost of $2.5 billion over 10 years.

"Add another $2.5 billion obligation into a strained state budget, that is a reason that so many of us from a purely practical financial point of view are concerned," he said. "We don’t have a luxury of having an unbalanced budget. Thankfully, we do have to balance our budget."

But Democrats and the Obama administration say repealing the law would put 4.3 million Georgians with pre-existing medical conditions in jeopardy of losing their insurance. Nationally, they say, as many as 129 million Americans with pre-existing conditions could lose their insurance.

Some 43,500 young Georgians would lose newly won rights to remain on their parents' insurance until age 26 if the legislation is repealed, according to Democrats. And Atlanta-area health care providers and taxpayers, they say, would continue to remain stuck with about $609 million in annual costs from providing care to the uninsured.

The U.S. House is expected to vote Wednesday to repeal the entire health care law. Given the GOP's new majority in the House, the vote is expected to fall along party lines. But Democrats still control the U.S. Senate and the White House, so any repeal effort is expected to end there.

But lawsuits filed by more than two dozen states continue to churn through the federal courts and could also eventually overturn all or part of the new policies. Georgia is one of the states suing to block the new law.
