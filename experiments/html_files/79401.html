Purchase of land in Cleveland's Flats clears way for new section of Towpath Trail

Print Email
Tonya Sams, The Plain Dealer By Tonya Sams, The Plain Dealer cleveland.com
on July 30, 2010 at 4:00 AM, updated July 30, 2010 at 11:33 AM
comments
View full sizeJohn Kuntz / The Plain DealerThe Ohio office of the Trust for Public Land has acquired some land in the Cleveland Flats area that will aid the completion of the Cuyahoga County section of the popular Ohio and Erie Canal Towpath.

CLEVELAND, Ohio -- Planners have added a piece of land in the Flats that's critical to finishing the Cuyahoga County section of the 101-mile Towpath Trail.

The Ohio Canal Corridor and the Trust for Public Land announced Thursday that they are acquiring 11 acres along the Cuyahoga River for $4.8 million.

The two adjacent properties will accommodate about a half-mile of trail and connect to the proposed Canal Basin Park, a 20-acre park at the site where the Ohio & Erie Canal once tied into the Cuyahoga River.

"This acquisition sends a clear message to any who doubt the future completion of the Towpath Trail in Cleveland," said Tim Donovan, Ohio Canal Corridor director, in a news release. "That message: It's happening. And it's happening now."

He said that the impact of the purchase will reach beyond the Towpath Trail by serving as a catalyst for a number of public and private developments percolating in the northern river valley. It will spur other bike and pedestrian trails linking to the Canal Basin Park.

The Ohio office of the Trust for Public Land has tried to purchase the land since 2007. The properties, near Scranton Road, were intended for riverside residential development. The landowners eventually decided to work with the Trust.

View full size

"This is a very important step in getting the Towpath completed and getting it into the Flats," Bill Carroll, state director for the Trust, said. "It's very exciting. It has taken a long time and it's been a complicated transaction. Now it's forever in public domain. It will always be a public space."

Money to buy the land came from the Clean Ohio Fund and from a Cuyahoga County state grant, according to a news release. The properties were purchased for $2.4 million each, and each purchase included $1.55 million from Clean Ohio and $850,000 from the county.

So far, 80 miles of the 101-mile ribbon of asphalt and crushed limestone are done. The trail will link the Lake Erie shoreline to Dover-New Philadelphia primarily following the route of the Ohio & Erie Canal.

The trail, parts of which opened in 1993, now ends at Harvard Avenue in Cleveland. Completing the trail the last six miles north toward Lake Erie has been difficult because of acquiring the land and dealing with obstacles, such as railroad tracks and industrial plants.
