Ohio Democrats defend their records in Congress

Posted on August 30, 2010 at 6:00 AM

By Sabrina Eaton, cleveland.com

seaton@cleveland.com

View full sizeSabrina Eaton, The Plain DealerRep. John Boccieri of Alliance meets with council members over breakfast in Louisville, Ohio. The freshman Democrat, who faces a tough re-election challenge, has been traveling his district to explain his votes on health care and other issues.

LOUISVILLE, Ohio - John Boccieri was in the hot seat at Mike's-Milann's restaurant in Louisville.

The freshman Democratic congressman from Alliance joined the city's mayor and council there in mid-August for a breakfast meeting where he delivered a rapid-fire sales pitch on what he and Congress have achieved over the past 20 months.

Seemingly without taking a breath, Boccieri spent eight minutes describing how the economic stimulus bill he endorsed stemmed job losses and helped reverse the nation's economic downturn, how energy legislation he supported would promote green jobs that can't be shipped to other countries, and how health-insurance reform legislation he backed will pare runaway costs.

Then the grilling began. Councilman Rick Guiley declared that the health bill will increase operating costs for small businesses and local governments, while Councilwoman Cheryle Casar questioned the wisdom of requiring people to buy health insurance if they don't want it.

"After your vote, my health insurance went up 57 percent immediately because of anticipated changes in the federal government," said Guiley, an attorney. "That is doing harm to me and doing harm to all these business owners."

"This is about making America stronger in the long run," Boccieri replied. "Health care costs at the end of the day will cripple this country. With respect to the rates going up, insurance companies are acting like the credit-card companies right now, frankly."

Like other Democrats from Ohio and around the nation, Boccieri spent much of August meeting with constituents to defend his voting record and answer questions about congressional efforts to stimulate the economy, promote energy self-sufficiency and reform the health-insurance system.

To judge from poll numbers, Boccieri and other Democrats in Ohio have an uphill battle heading into November elections that could shift the House of Representatives into GOP hands. A poll released Thursday by the GOP-leaning American Action Forum showed Boccieri's Republican challenger, Wadsworth businessman Jim Renacci, with a 14-point lead over the incumbent.

Similar telephone surveys of 400 likely voters that the group conducted in Ohio found GOP candidates with smaller leads over freshman Democratic incumbents Steve Driehaus in Cincinnati and Mary Jo Kilroy in Columbus. Sophomore Democratic Rep. Betty Sutton of Copley Township had a 2-point lead over Republican Tom Ganley, a Brecksville auto dealer, in the American Action Forum poll conducted by Ayres, McHenry & Associates.

Although Boccieri's campaign said the poll numbers are biased because they come from a Republican group, the nonpartisan Rothenberg Political Report rates his race as a "pure toss-up." It says the Driehaus and Kilroy contests "lean Republican" and Sutton's race leans Democrat.

Renacci says voters are concerned about jobs and the economy and don't think Boccieri's votes reflect their views. He says they're particularly mad that Boccieri voted against an early version of the health-insurance reform bill but supported its final draft and feel he "will tell them one thing and then vote a different way."

"I was at a meet-and-greet fundraiser in Ashland County the other day," Renacci said as he shook hands at a North Canton Rotary Club meeting. "It was the largest Ashland County fundraiser ever for a political candidate. Why is that? Because they are mad. They are concerned. People said to me, 'I have never got involved in politics before, but I am here now.' "

Boccieri concedes he has work to do in explaining his health-care vote in parts of his district, but he maintains he has solid chances for re-election despite the poor economy and the historic trend of the party that controls the White House losing congressional seats in midterm elections.

"This election will come down to a choice: Do we want to go back to the same policies that got us into this ditch or continue growing our way out of it?" Boccieri says. He also contends Renacci's past legal disputes, including a $1.4 million back-tax assessment by the Ohio Department of Taxation, will detract from his candidacy. Renacci attributes publicity about his legal battles to a Democratic Party "smear campaign" and hands voters business cards that list his cell phone number so he can answer questions about the disputes.

Incumbent acknowledges

Americans' anxiety

As he greets voters at church festivals and union picnics in the Cincinnati area, Driehaus hears some of the same questions as Boccieri about the economy and his health care vote.

"There is a groundswell of anxiety," says Driehaus, who is running against Steve Chabot, the former Republican congressman he defeated in 2008. "Some people want to be able to blame someone, and Congress is an easy target."

In the Columbus area, Kilroy faces a tough rematch with the Republican she narrowly defeated in 2008, Steve Stivers. Kilroy says that voters are concerned about the economy and the grip that Wall Street and other special interests have on Washington but that they believe things are starting to improve. Without the stimulus bill that she supported, she says, unemployment would be more severe.

"In December of 2008, right before I took office, we lost 670,000 jobs in this country," she said in an interview before meeting with veterans in her Columbus office. "We have reversed that trend. We are gaining jobs each month, instead of shedding jobs at huge rates. We will continue to have that kind of focus because it is hugely important that American families be able to enjoy a middle-class lifestyle, enjoy the American Dream, have a house that isn't in foreclosure and be able to send their children to college. We will get there, and we will continue to make that happen."

Sutton is similarly upbeat, saying that voters she's met support efforts by Congress to level the playing field for Northeast Ohio and provide greater economic opportunities.

"I have confidence in the people and the voters," Sutton says. "I know they appreciate people who stand up and fight for them, even during these challenging times."

Still, Sutton made sure to tell participants in a 75th-anniversary party for Social Security at an Elyria senior citizens center that John Boehner, the House GOP leader from southwest Ohio, has expressed interest in raising the retirement age for Social Security. And she let them know that Boehner -- who would become the next House speaker if the GOP takes control -- supports her opponent.

"Tell everybody you know what is at stake, because there are very clear differences about who stands where," Sutton said.

'We have a lot of work

ahead of us'

After leaving the Louisville restaurant, Boccieri visited a day-care center, a pharmacy and Metzger's Ace Hardware, where customer Dave Kennedy accosted him about his health-care vote. Kennedy said he heard calls to Boccieri's office ran 2-to-1 against the bill, and he asked how he could support the bill under those circumstances.

"I have a real concern about where we are headed and how we are spending money we don't have," said Kennedy, a teacher.

Boccieri replied that most calls to his office were "robocalls" from outside his district and that he thought the bill was in the country's best interests because it would save the nation $1.3 trillion in the next 20 years and help bring down health-care costs that threaten the nation's economic security.

"We have a lot of work ahead of us," Boccieri told him.

Although Kennedy seemed unimpressed by Boccieri's response, Massillon homemaker Ann Schumacher, who won the opportunity to accompany Boccieri on his swing through Louisville in a church auction, said she enthusiastically backs the health-care bill because she has trouble finding affordable health insurance after having undergone a kidney transplant.

"Seeing Congressman Boccieri made me feel I voted for the right person, whether he wins or loses," Schumacher said. "He is concerned, compassionate and sincere. He seems like the kind of person that you could sit at the kitchen table and talk with for hours."
