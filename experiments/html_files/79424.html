Cleveland Councilman Mike Polensek says drastic sewer increase necessary to clean up Lake Erie

Print Email
Michael Scott, The Plain Dealer By Michael Scott, The Plain Dealer cleveland.com
Email the author | Follow on Twitter
on November 18, 2010 at 8:30 PM, updated November 19, 2010 at 11:45 AM
comments
Lisa DeJong, The Plain DealerCleveland City Councilman Mike Polensek told Northeast Ohio Regional Sewer District officials Thursday that he favors a $3 billion plan to reduce pollution into Lake Erie, even if it means financial hardship for his ward.
CLEVELAND, Ohio -- Cleveland City Councilman Mike Polensek told regional sewer officials Thursday that he understands both sides of an increasingly dramatic debate over a pending $3 billion settlement with EPA that will triple sewer rates in the city and more than 60 suburbs.

Polensek said he supports the massive rate increase, even though it will likely be especially hard on already economically challenged residents and businesses in his ward in the Collinwood area.

But he also said the mandated construction work will finally result in something for which he's been fighting for years: A swimmable Lake Erie along the two miles of shoreline in Polensek's ward.

"Let's face it, this is the year 2010 and we're worse than a Third World country in terms of water quality and the way we treat our sewage," Polensek told Northeast Ohio Regional Sewer District trustees. "What we're paying for now is really an indictment of the 40-year delay in living up to the Clean Water Act."

Previous Plain Dealer coverage

  • All our coverage on the Regional Sewer District

Sewer officials this month announced they reached a tentative settlement with the U.S. EPA, Ohio EPA and U.S. Department of Justice to reduce pollution going into Lake Erie to meet federal requirements by 2035.

The board is expected to vote on the plan at its Dec. 2 meeting. Also on the agenda that day will be the first piece of the plan: The $198 million Euclid Creek Tunnel, the project for which Polensek said he has lobbied for a decade.

Combined sewers overflow into Euclid Creek about 60 times a year, on average, sewer officials said. That makes the tributary the most polluted in the region.

"My dog, a lab, won't even drink the water from Euclid Creek after a heavy rain," Polensek said. "It's that disgusting and even he knows better."

The sewer district also plans to build another half-dozen massive underground tunnels in Northeast Ohio to catch sewage overflows. Since 1972, the district has reduced by half, to 4.5 billion gallons a year, the amount of untreated human waste and storm water runoff going into Lake Erie.

The construction of the tunnels and other improvements is expected cut that sewage overflow down to about 500 million (or half a billion) gallons a year.

Polensek was one of about a half dozen people who spoke at the sewer board's regular meeting Thursday. Laundromat owner Gary Stein of Middleburg Heights, spoke against the rate hike. He said the cost to small businesses and residents could be crushing.

Stein said he's afraid he'll have to charge his customers more to use his equipment because his sewer bill is tied directly to water use.

Sewer rates in Cleveland and more than 60 suburbs, including Middleburg Heights, have been rising by about 9 percent a year for several years.

The new proposal calls for 13 percent annual increases from 2012-2016 and then nearly 6 percent jumps annually through 2035 to pay for the $3 billion program. Ultimately, the average ratepayer could end up shelling out $1,000 a year -- triple the average bill today.

Board member Gary Starr, mayor of Middleburg Heights, has argued that an increase of this size is reason enough to delay the EPA agreement until residents and public officials can prepare for it. But Executive Director Julius Ciaccia has said it would cost another $100 million in inflation for every year of additional delay.

Sewer district staff also showed an hourlong presentation of the proposed settlement to a crowd of about 75 people. Go to neorsd.org and click on "Project Clean Lake" to see the entire plan.
