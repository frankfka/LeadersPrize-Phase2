Walker's business tax cut plan advances

By Jason Stein of the Journal Sentinel
Jan. 20, 2011
  • Tweet
  • Email
  • Print
  • (0) Comments
Related Coverage

  • Walker's business tax cut plan advances

Madison — Republicans on Thursday advanced out of the budget committee the latest version of Gov. Scott Walker's tax cut plan for businesses, setting up a possible vote on the bill by the Assembly on Tuesday.

On a party-line, 12-4 vote, the Joint Finance Committee approved changing the proposal to make the tax cut available to a broader range of businesses at less of a cost overall.

Under the new version, businesses with sales of up to $5 million annually would receive a tax deduction of $4,000 for each job they create. Businesses with sales of more than $5 million a year would receive a deduction of $2,000 for each job they create.

The proposal would save businesses an estimated $33.5 million in taxes and cost the state that amount in lost revenue. With the state facing an up to $3.3 billion shortfall in the 2011-'13 budget, passing the proposal would mean lawmakers and Walker would have to find more spending cuts.

"That's our job today - not to pick winners and losers but to put Wisconsin residents to work," said Rep. John Nygren (R-Marinette).

The proposed tax cut - part of a special legislative session called by Walker on job creation - has already gone through several different forms. On the campaign trail, Walker said he would shave 1 percentage point off the income taxes of businesses with 50 or fewer employees. But after he was elected, he replaced that idea with a plan to give a tax break to businesses with $500,000 or less in gross sales. That idea would cost the state $40 million a year in revenue.

Mom-and-pop operations said that proposal would provide them a tax break, but others said it would be hard for some employers with as few as 10 workers to qualify for it.

Joint Finance co-chairman Robin Vos (R-Caledonia) had also floated his own proposal for a tax deduction of $1,000 per job created.

The latest tax cut proposal draws on elements from both Walker's and Vos' measures and could potentially apply to corporate income taxes or personal income taxes, depending on how the business is structured.

The number of jobs a business had created would be determined by comparing the total number of workers reported with the state's unemployment insurance program over time.

Businesses could apply the state tax break going back to Jan. 1 of this year.

It would amount to between $92 and $316 per job, depending on how the business owner files his or her taxes and what tax rate applies to him or her, according to an analysis by the nonpartisan Legislative Fiscal Bureau. For instance, the state corporate tax rate is 7.9% and the state individual income tax rate varies from 4.6% to 7.75%, depending on the taxpayer's income.

The modest amount in state tax savings per job would be partially offset by an increase in federal taxes. That's because taxpayers can deduct their state taxes paid from their federal income taxes. As a result, any decrease in their state taxes also decreases their federal deductions.

Democrats questioned whether the small savings would lead businesses to hire more workers and said the state couldn't afford to approve it with its budget already severely stressed.

"How is this . . .  going to change the behavior of a small business in northern Wisconsin?" Sen. Bob Jauch (D-Poplar) asked.

On party-line votes, Republicans defeated several amendments offered by Democrats, who said those changes would have provided more accountability through steps such as requiring an audit of its effectiveness.

But Sen. Randy Hopper (R-Fond du Lac) said such an audit wouldn't be necessary. That's because he said he wants to seek a yearly audit of the state's economic development programs as part of a bill to partly privatize the Department of Commerce.

That bill is currently before the Senate economic development committee chaired by Hopper.
