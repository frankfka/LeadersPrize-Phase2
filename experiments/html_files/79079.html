Pension borrowing plan back

County needs state OK to issue bonds

By Steven Schultze of the Journal Sentinel
Jan. 21, 2008

  • Section: Pension Scandal

A plan to borrow money and invest the proceeds as a way of managing Milwaukee County's pension debt - an idea once rejected by voters and reviled by critics as too risky - now appears headed for fast-track approval.

County officials are banking on winning a quick OK from the Legislature so they can float $267 million in pension obligation bonds and avoid having to sink more cash this year into the pension fund. The sum represents the county's unfunded pension liability - the difference between the $1.6 billion already in the county pension fund and the amount needed to cover expected retirees' pensions.

The legislation is needed primarily to allow the county to issue 30-year bonds to pay for its unfunded pension costs. The county already has authority for issuing 20-year bonds.

The new plan is a revision of one first floated four years ago by County Executive Scott Walker. As with the original plan, the county would borrow millions to cover the county's pension obligation to its retirees and invest the proceeds at a higher rate.

The difference between borrowing costs and what the county could earn investing the borrowed money would be an estimated $90 million, according to county figures.

A preliminary outline developed by a special county panel suggests borrowing the $267 million at 6% interest, with total payback costs of $616 million. Using an 8% figure for expected return on investing the borrowed money means it would generate $706 million, for the $90 million saving.

The county would pay off its pension debt with annual payments of $21 million, under the plan outline.

Protective elements

New safeguards added to the pension bond idea are aimed at reducing risks and reassuring state officials whose approval is needed. Chief among them: The county would create a reserve fund to help counter unexpected increases in annual pension costs, and the state could cut the county's annual state aid if the county failed to make its full annual pension payment.

Walker said the new pension bond plan addresses concerns raised about past versions. He said he was optimistic state legislation could be passed by March, when the current session ends, and the county could take action shortly after that.

After state approval, Walker and the County Board would have to firm up details on how the bond plan would work.

That timeframe would allow the county to take advantage of current low interest rates for borrowers, he said.

Walker's 2008 budget counts on $11 million, or 12% of the anticipated pension bond saving, to help fund this year's pension costs.

Risks cited

Some concerns remain, however.

"You are basically gambling on the rate of return" from investing the borrowed money, said veteran County Supervisor Richard Nyklewicz Jr. "You are playing the margin and the markets."

Despite reservations, Nyklewicz said he had confidence the financial experts the county has tapped would properly scrutinize any plan that would go before the County Board.

Nyklewicz also said using the savings from the plan in its early years would be a mistake. Walker's earlier plan envisioned using a much bigger chunk of bond proceeds.

State Sen. Glenn Grothman (R-West Bend) said he remained leery of the borrow-and-invest plan but might wind up voting for the legislation anyway.

"Would you take out a mortgage on your house at 6 percent to buy stock?" he asked. But the state legislation would force Milwaukee County to make full annual pension payments - an important safeguard, he said.

County officials argue that it should be allowed to do what the state and City of Milwaukee have already done. Issuing pension bonds has become fairly common in other states, as well.

A government finance trade group recommends caution in how such plans are fashioned. Principal debt payments should not be deferred, and governments that borrow to pay pension debt may still face future pension liabilities because of changes in benefit levels or uneven investment returns, according to the Government Finance Officers Association.

Two legislative panels have reviewed the legislation and are expected to advance it this week.

Walker is optimistic Gov. Jim Doyle will sign the pension bond legislation into law. He noted that the governor included pension bonding authority for the county in his 2008-'09 state budget proposal, but legislators removed it for separate consideration.

Doyle spokesman Matt Canter said the governor remains supportive but would have to see details of any plan that reached his desk before committing to signing it.
