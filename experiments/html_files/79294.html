State working join federal health lawsuit, Van Hollen says

Joining Florida case will require federal judge's OK

  • e-mail
  • print
By Jason Stein of the Journal Sentinel

Jan. 10, 2011 |(195) Comments

Madison Wisconsin's attorney general is seeking to join counterparts from other states suing to overturn the federal health law, focusing on a provision requiring Americans to purchase health insurance.

Attorney General J.B. Van Hollen, a Republican, said Monday that Florida Attorney General Pam Bondi will request this week that Wisconsin and at least three other states be allowed to join the federal case, joining 20 more states that are already a part of it. U.S. District Judge Roger Vinson in Pensacola, Fla., would then have to decide whether to grant the request.

"Mandating that an individual buy something solely by virtue of their existence goes so far that it effectively eliminates any restrictions on the federal government's powers," Van Hollen said in an interview.

The legal action is one of the immediate consequences of Wisconsin Republicans taking back the governor's office and Legislature from Democrats in November's elections.

Van Hollen needs the approval of either the governor or one house of the Legislature to sue on behalf of the state. In March he sought such authority in the health care case from Gov. Jim Doyle and his fellow Democrats in the Legislature and was rejected, with Doyle calling the lawsuit a "frivolous and political attempt to thwart the actions of Congress."

But once Republican Gov. Scott Walker took office last week, one of his first actions was to authorize Van Hollen to sue.

The sweeping law generated intense controversy in the run-up to its passage last year and continues to do so, with newly ascendant Republicans in the U.S. House planning a vote soon to repeal the law. That effort is expected to fail in the U.S. Senate, which is still controlled by Democrats.

Democrats critical

State Democratic Party chairman Mike Tate said Van Hollen's move, if successful, could deny health care to "tens of thousands" of Wisconsin residents who could receive coverage in the coming years through the health law. He questioned whether the lawsuit would end up costing the state money or cutting down on the time that state Department of Justice attorneys can work on other priorities, such as handling criminal appeals cases.

"He must now be explicit about what costs there will be to Wisconsin taxpayers when their lawyers are doing Scott Walker's partisan bidding, instead of fighting crime," Tate said of Van Hollen in a statement.

Van Hollen said he expected the cost of the case to be minimal and would be far outweighed by the importance of the issue. He said he would assign two attorneys to the case on a "very part-time" basis, because the majority of the legal work would be handled by states that are already a part of the lawsuit.

Other issues in the case include whether the federal government can require states to cover more low-income people under Medicaid health care programs that jointly are paid for by the states and federal government. Wisconsin programs such as BadgerCare Plus already cover poor children, families and childless adults at higher income levels than those mandated under the federal law. But the federal law could still make it more difficult for Walker and the Republican Legislature to cut state health programs - a rapidly rising cost for the state - to help balance a massive state budget deficit over the next two years.

Once the major provisions of the federal law take effect in 2014, the state could receive additional federal money for BadgerCare Plus. Doyle's administration estimated last year that between January 2014 and June 2016 the state could save $364.6 million under the health care law.

State Rep. Jon Richards (D-Milwaukee) said that amounted to "big, big money" for the state. But Walker has said repeatedly that the state needs to have maximum flexibility with its health programs to be able to balance its budget without raising taxes.

A federal judge in Virginia ruled in December in a separate case that the mandate on purchasing insurance is unconstitutional. Two other federal judges have upheld the requirement, putting it on a likely track for the U.S. Supreme Court.
