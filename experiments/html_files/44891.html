Senate Passes Patch for Highway Trust Fund

With the House out of town, the three-month extension buys lawmakers more time to come up with a long-term fix.

By Gabrielle Levy, Political Reporter |July 30, 2015, at 4:19 p.m.

Senate Passes Patch for Highway Trust Fund

Congress will have to tackle a long-term solution for highway funding in the fall after lawmakers could only agree on a short-term fix this week. (Getty Images)

Congress has managed to avoid a shutdown of highway funding for another three months, but pushed the difficult questions of how to pay for the nation's road and rail projects long-term into an already bursting fall.

The Senate on Thursday passed a pair of measures: one to authorize the Highway Trust Fund for six years with three years of funding and a second, three-month extension already passed by the House to keep the fund afloat. The Senate passed the House patch ostensibly to buy time for both congressional chambers to come to a long-term agreement.

Related Content

Senate to Act on Short-Term Highway Bill

[RELATED: Senate to Act on Short-Term Highway Bill]

"There is an overriding obligation here" to get a long-term bill done, Sen. Barbara Boxer, D-Calif., said before the vote on the shorter extension. 

Without the three-month stopgap, authorization for the Eisenhower-era program – which provides money through the federal fuel tax to states and localities to build and maintain the nation's roads and bridges, as well as public transportation systems – would have run out Friday. Lawmakers now face an Oct. 29 deadline to keep the fund up and running after approving an $8 billion transfer from the U.S. Treasury.

The six-year bill, negotiated by Boxer, Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell and Sen. Jim Inhofe, R-Okla., only identifies funding sources for three years, prompting some bipartisan criticism. It also includes a measure to reauthorize the expired charter of the Export-Import Bank, which appears to have the support of a significant majority in Congress but has been tripped up by opposition from conservatives and House Republican leaders.

Sixty-five senators voted in favor of the six-year measure while 34 voted against – a margin Boxer and Inhofe agreed was huge in an era of intense partisanship and one they believe sent a strong signal to the House lawmakers working to produce their own long-term bill. The short-term extension sailed through even more easily, 91 to 4.

Most of the opposition to the long-term bill came from senators opposed to its myriad sources of funding, which aim to make up what the Congressional Budget Office has projected will be a shortfall of about $13 billion this year and more in later years. Others were wary that the bill was not fully funded for all six years.

Inhofe and Boxer told reporters Thursday that while they were disappointed in another short-term extension – the 34th in the last decade – the next three months will buy time for the House and Senate to put their heads together to find money for the second half of the bill's authorization period. Some House lawmakers, for example, have pushed for a kind of corporate tax reform known as repatriation, which would tax the overseas profits of U.S.-based companies.

"They're going to be busy going after other sources," Inhofe said. "It's hard to come up with anything that's going to be sellable to everybody."

"If nothing happens, work will stop [after three years]," he said. "That's the pressure that will be put on all of us."

Related Content

Road To Long-Term Highway Bill Closed in House

[RELATED: Road To Long-Term Highway Bill Closed in House]


Earlier this week, House leaders said they were not interested in considering the Senate bill in its current form. They preferred a five-month extension of funds, pushing the deadline to mid-December, but ultimately agreed to shorten the period to three months.

The House left town Wednesday after passing the extension by an overwhelming vote, but House Transportation Committee Chairman Bill Shuster, R-Pa., committed to finishing work on a long-term bill "as soon as possible."

"This legislation continues to fund road, bridge, and other infrastructure improvements for America while providing the House time to put forward a fiscally responsible long-term surface transportation proposal when Congress returns and then go to conference with the Senate," he said in a statement following the vote. "We all share the same goal of completing a long-term bill as soon as possible, and ensuring that critical programs do not shut down before we achieve that goal is the right thing to do."

Congress will have a full plate when it gets back from recess after Labor Day, when it will be tasked with continuing to fund the government, negotiating under threat of a shutdown and voting on the agreement to limit Iran's nuclear program.

"We have a lot of unfinished business," House Minority Leader Nancy Pelosi said Thursday morning, with a tinge of sarcasm. Speaker John Boehner "predicts that September's going to be this great month."
