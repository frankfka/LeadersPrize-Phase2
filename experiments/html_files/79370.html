The House's online, open transition

  • 11 Comments
  • RSS
  • Email
  • Print
The author writes that Republicans will keep their word when the new Congress convenes. | AP Photo Close
By REP. GREG WALDEN | 1/5/11 4:39 AM EST

The American people sent Congress a clear message in November: Focus on creating jobs, stop the out-of-control spending and reduce government size.

In the House Republicans’ Pledge to America, crafted with input from the American people, we promised to do just that. And we will keep our word when the new Congress convenes on Wednesday

  • -
  • +
  • reset

POLITICO 44

One important part of the pledge is a promise to restore trust in Congress by making it more open and accountable.

After all, it is the people’s House, the public’s business and the taxpayers’ money — and they should have the right to watch and participate in the process.

Speaker-elect John Boehner (R-Ohio) asked me to lead the transition team to implement reforms and oversee a smooth change-over, so the House can begin addressing the people’s priorities at the start of the 112th Congress.

The morning after Election Day, I organized a team of 22 lawmakers to re-write the rules that govern how the House does business and to scour the Capitol Hill operations for inefficiencies.

The effort was unprecedented —both in terms of size and inclusiveness. Four members of the team were incoming freshmen, who bring a new perspective and energy. I asked Speaker Nancy Pelosi (D-C alif.) to designate two Democratic participants, an act of bipartisanship missing from previous transitions. The transition team also received suggestions from thousands of Americans, who submitted ideas through our website.

The team approached the challenge of reforming a centuries-old institution with a simple premise: For too long, both parties have run Congress the wrong way. The backroom deals, the rushed multi-thousand page bills and the explosion in spending borrowed money happened too frequently — under the leadership of both parties.

In December, we unveiled our new rules plan, requiring all bills to be posted online in a searchable format for at least three days before a vote on the House floor. No longer will bills be dropped in the middle of the night, then voted on the next day.

That’s just the start of the reforms. We will require that all bills include a citation of constitutional authority — so that Congress respects the limits imposed by our founding document.

To begin to control the explosion in spending, we’re clamping down on the budgetary sleight-of-hands that hide spending beyond the first 10-year window of a bill. Any legislation projected to increase the deficit by more than $5 billion in any single 10-year window, out to 50 years, will be subject to a point of order.

The House will adopt a new “cut-go” rule, which requires any suspension bill that increases authorizations or creates new programs to make equal or greater cuts elsewhere.
