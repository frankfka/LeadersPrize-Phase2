Wisconsin a place to expand, Walker tells Chicagoans

'I'm not here to poach business,' he says to civic group

Wisconsin Gov. Scott Walker speaks at a news conference in Chicago Monday after his speech to the Commercial Club of Chicago. Credit: Associated Press
June 25, 2012
0 Comments
SHARE

By Bill Glauber of the Journal Sentinel

June 25, 2012 0

Chicago - Gov. Scott Walker came to Illinois Monday to tout his government reforms, talk pensions and tout Wisconsin to some of Chicago's more influential business leaders.

"I'm not here to poach business," Walker said during a news conference after giving a speech to the Commercial Club of Chicago, a key civic group.

Walker said he was trying to make the case "if you're looking for places to expand or grow in, Wisconsin is a great place to do it."

In January 2011, Walker brought up the old slogan "Escape to Wisconsin" to lure businesses to the state after the Illinois legislature passed a tax increase.

And during the recent recall campaign, Walker often contrasted how he was able to close a budget deficit largely without raising taxes, while Illinois raised taxes and was still burdened with a deficit.

"Long term, it's in our best interest in Wisconsin to have a strong Illinois, particularly a strong Chicago," Walker said. As one of the world's megacities, Chicago is a key driver in international trade, Walker said.

Walker spoke to around 150 business leaders. The speech was closed to the media, which is customary for the Commercial Club.

Miles White, chairman and chief executive officer of Abbott Laboratories, and the current chairman of the Commercial Club, said Walker was invited "to talk about the tough but fiscally responsible decisions he has made for his state."

"Obviously, those decisions were not easy and not everyone was pleased, but they needed to be done," White said.

Walker, who led the fight to curtail collective bargaining for most public-sector employees while also getting them to contribute more for health care and pensions, said "every state, every jurisdiction is unique, so what works in Wisconsin won't necessarily be an automatic carbon copy" for other states.

Last week, the Pew Center on the States released a report showing that in fiscal 2010, before Walker took office, Wisconsin was the only state in the country to have a fully funded state pension system. Illinois has funded only 45% of its pension obligations and ranked last in the nation.

Walker said part of the reason for Wisconsin's success in funding the pension "is a pay as you go model."

A report on pensions for state and local workers is due to be released later this month by the Walker administration and state Department of Employee Trust Funds.

"What I've said is I'm not proposing any changes at this time, it doesn't mean that I wouldn't be open to them," Walker said.

He added, "I'm not touching the pension for current retirees."

Asked about any contingency plans for the state after Thursday's expected ruling by the U.S. Supreme Court on the Affordable Care Act, Walker said if the court upheld the law, "that certainly would stop the legal option."

"Before the state took any other action, we'd wait to see after November what the political ramifications are in the presidential and the U.S. Senate races," Walker said.

He added:

"The biggest thing we can do is work on ways to require greater transparency that would have a real impact on driving down health care costs without an individual mandate."

When asked about a Journal Sentinel story that revealed two Wisconsin billionaires, Helen Johnson-Leipold and Diane Hendricks, paid no state income tax in 2010, Walker said it "goes to the larger argument of simplifying the federal tax code."

"If you just had something simple, something close to a flat tax, then you'd get rid of all the exemptions and exceptions," he said. "Ideally, a lower rate, then across the board. That would be easier."

"If they put a flat tax at the federal level, then we could apply the same thing at the state level," he added.
