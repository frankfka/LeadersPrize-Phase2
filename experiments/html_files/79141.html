Milwaukee County

Changes keep Milwaukee County health costs down

By Steve Schultze of the Journal Sentinel

Posted: April 30, 2010 |(14) Comments

Click to enlarge

Milwaukee County's employee health care costs have doubled over the past decade to almost $118 million last year, a major contributor to the budget crunch the county now faces.

But just under the surface of that grim statistic lies another story - the reversal of a one-year fling with buying health insurance coverage to a self-insured program. The move has reined in increases that would have had an even more crippling effect on services. Absent those savings, the county would be faced with layoffs of hundreds more workers, said Steven Kreklow, the county budget director.

Reverting to a self-insured system - coupled with negotiated savings with a new health care administrator, new wellness programs and higher employee costs - has kept cost increases $100 million lower than they otherwise would have risen, county officials said.

The $100 million figure covers reduced costs for 2006 through 2009.

The trend has provided some breathing room and political cover in a budget that otherwise has become an ever-tightening straitjacket, as layoffs, furloughs and spending freezes have dominated the news.

The health insurance changes included an estimated $20 million in lower costs in 2009, for reasons not yet fully understood but nonetheless appreciated.

"This was a true ray of sunshine for us," said David Arena, the county's employee benefits manager.

The county had been counting on a health insurance savings of about $8 million or $9 million for 2009. The greater savings allowed County Executive Scott Walker last month to announce a year-end surplus of nearly $9 million, something he's touting in his Republican bid for governor.

The county's health care dividend has spawned an unusual round of congratulation in an often prickly political environment. Walker and County Board Chairman Lee Holloway each credit the other - in addition to themselves - for seeking changes that have paid off in lower costs.

Walker and Holloway agreed to appoint a study group of county staffers in mid-2006 after county auditors raised concerns about a health care contract the county had signed less than a year earlier. Holloway also pushed through a bureaucratic change creating a separate Employee Benefits Division, to raise the county's emphasis and expertise on health care costs.

The 2006 move first shifted the county to outside insurance coverage for its lower-cost HMO plan and its more traditional preferred provider plan. The rationale for the shift was to get the predictability of annual cost boosts set by contract with WPS Health Insurance, but the deal included annual increases that seemed exorbitant, said County Auditor Jerome Heer.

"We traded off good pricing for predictability," Heer said, which he called "a bad decision."

With rising concern over the deal, the county brought in a new consultant to review its insurance setup. Stuart Piltch and his firm, Cambridge Advisory Group, played a key role in the study committee and in subsequent negotiations in which the county exited its WPS deal, hired United Health Care and converted back to being self-insured, according to Heer.

The insurance pact he retooled "was not a well-conceived deal," Piltch said.

The county has paid Cambridge more than $800,000 for its work on health care, and the firm also has been hired to review 2009 insurance claims to better understand reasons for the unexpectedly large savings. Kreklow said it's not clear yet how much of the savings might be random - caused, for example, by fewer expensive illnesses among workers and retirees.

Wellness provisions added in 2008 also were credited with helping control costs and with having potential for additional savings. Richard Abelson, executive director of the county's largest union, said the push for that came from the union and was resisted initially by county negotiators.

Piltch said there's potential for more savings, perhaps through greater use of wellness programs and a more competitive market for a third-party administrator that handles the county's health claims.

Walker is pushing for union concessions in the form of higher employee health insurance costs that would also lower future costs, said Kreklow.
