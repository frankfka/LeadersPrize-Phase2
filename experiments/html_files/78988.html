Walker signs HSA measure; budget repair bill planned

By Patrick Marley and Jason Stein of the Journal Sentinel
Jan. 25, 2011
  • Tweet
  • Email
  • Print
  • (0) Comments

DeForest Gov. Scott Walker signed his first bill Monday - providing tax breaks for those with health savings accounts - but also warned the public that the state's fiscal condition is so bad that lawmakers must take action before passing a new two-year budget.

That means before Walker introduces his 2011-'13 budget next month, he will roll out a plan to close a gap in the fiscal year that ends June 30. Walker declined to say just how big the short-term deficit is.

The bill Walker signed is part of a special legislative session focused on jobs that Walker called hours after he was sworn in Jan. 3. Republicans argue HSAs make people more conscious of cost, which can help control overall health-care costs. Democrats said the measure would add to the state's budget problems without creating jobs.

At a bill signing ceremony at EVCO Plastics in DeForest, Walker said the tax break marks the beginning of an "aggressive legislative agenda."

"This is just our first step," he said. "This is not the final step for small business."

Republicans have long pushed for providing tax breaks for HSAs, but they were thwarted by Democrats in the Legislature and former Democratic Gov. Jim Doyle. Now that the GOP controls both houses and the governor's office, the Republicans passed it easily.

The next state budget faces a shortfall of more than $3 billion, and the health savings accounts bill will add $49 million to that, according to the nonpartisan Legislative Fiscal Bureau. But first, Walker will have to propose cuts for the current fiscal year.

The size of this year's budget problem will be spelled out before Walker gives his "state of the state" address on Feb. 1, he said. That will give him a chance to explain how he wants to fix the state's finances, both immediately and for the longer term.

A pair of reports in November and December by Doyle's administration projected that the state would have a of about $67 million on a budget of $13.8 billion this year in the state's main account.

If costs turn out to be higher than expected or revenues lower than expected, a budget repair bill could be automatically triggered by law. That would happen if the budget is put out of whack by roughly $136 million or more. Even if that doesn't happen, Walker can still introduce a bill.

The budget has been under a range of recent pressures that could trip the legal trigger requiring a bill:

The state's Medicaid health programs for the poor, prisons and public defender's office together are running about $223 million over budget, according to a summary released by Walker's transition office on Dec. 28.

Bad bookkeeping puffed up the state's reporting of its financial reserves last year by $45.3 million, a legislative audit reported last week.

The state also is under a court order to pay back a fund to compensate injured patients for $200 million in illegal transfers, though that money may not be due until the budget cycle that begins July 1.

Minnesota is demanding Wisconsin immediately pay $58 million under a tax reciprocity deal Minnesota canceled in 2009.

A wild card is whether state tax collections are performing better or worse than projected.

The HSA bill would allow people to deduct contributions to such accounts from their state income taxes, as they can their federal income taxes. It would apply to this tax year, for filings due in April 2012.

Until Monday, Wisconsin was one of four states that didn't provide tax breaks for the accounts.

HSAs are paired with health plans that have deductibles of at least $1,200 for an individual and $2,400 for family coverage. Because of the high deductibles, they have lower premiums than other plans.

The money put into the accounts is exempt from federal taxes - and now state taxes - if it is used for health care expenses.

Use of HSAs has grown rapidly nationally since Congress created the accounts in 2003, but relatively few people in Wisconsin have them. In 2008, about 35,300 Wisconsinites, or 1.3% of state tax filers, claimed the federal deduction for health savings accounts, according to the fiscal bureau. The average deduction was $2,586, which would translate to an average state tax savings of $168.

The tax savings in many cases will be offset by increased health-care costs to meet the high deductibles in plans associated with HSAs, said Rep. Sandy Pasch (D-Whitefish Bay).

The idea behind HSAs is to make people more conscious of the cost of health care because their own money is at stake. That, in turn, is meant to spark more comparison shopping and help bring overall costs down.
