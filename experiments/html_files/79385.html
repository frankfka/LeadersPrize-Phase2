Wauwatosa approves rezoning for UWM campus at County Grounds

By Tom Daykin of the Journal Sentinel
May 5, 2010

The University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee's development plan for a portion of the Milwaukee County Grounds has won approval, marking another step forward in the university's growth agenda.

The Wauwatosa Common Council voted 13-2 to approve UWM's preliminary plans to develop a science research facility, technology-based business park and other buildings, including housing created within the historic Eschweiler buildings. The council also voted 15-0 to approve zoning for the development, to be called Innovation Park.

A portion of the site, east of Highway 45 and north of W. Watertown Plank Road, will be set aside as a wildlife habitat, including migration grounds for Monarch butterflies.

UWM will return for additional city approvals on more detailed portions of the overall plan as the university develops various portions of the property.

But Tuesday night's vote paves the way for a university affiliate to buy the land.

The County Board a year ago approved an agreement to sell the 89-acre parcel for $13.55 million. The buyer will be UWM Innovation Park LLC, an affiliate of the university's real estate foundation. It has until Sept. 1 to close on the purchase.

University officials say they still plan to develop the site, despite the loss of funding they had expected to receive for the project from retired business executive Michael Cudahy.

Fund raising is continuing, and UWM officials are optimistic they will be ready to close on the purchase by September, said Tom Luljak, vice chancellor of university relations.

The university will need $5million to make its first payment for the land, with another $5 million coming a year later, said David Gilbert, special adviser to UWM Chancellor Carlos Santiago. The remaining payments would be made over the following four years.

UWM has "a large group" of donors for Innovation Park, Gilbert said, including some who were waiting to make sure the project received zoning approval before writing their checks. He said the university is not ready to name those donors.

UWM originally planned to purchase the land by April. The closing date was extended to September because it took longer than expected for university officials to complete the wildlife habitat plan. That plan had to be finished before the university could seek zoning approval.

Spring start

The university plans to begin building streets and other public improvements on the land in the spring of 2011, Gilbert said. The schedule for building campus facilities will depend on obtaining state funding as well as outside grants.

Supporters of the development say the planned Wauwatosa campus could provide UWM with world-class research facilities. That would improve the university's ability to obtain large research grants, such as those provided by the National Institutes of Health, while enabling its researchers to collaborate with scientists at the nearby Medical College of Wisconsin.

Opponents say putting engineering facilities a 30-minute drive from UWM's main campus on the east side would strain the ability of faculty to develop research partnerships with their colleagues in related fields.

Innovation Park is part of UWM's larger growth plan.

The school plans to:

Develop a School of Public Health at the former Pabst brewery downtown.

Develop new , which might be split between the Milwaukee harbor area and the Reed Street Yards site, near the Harley-Davidson Museum. Develop an integrated research facility at the northwest corner of N. Maryland Ave. and E. Kenwood Blvd. Purchase of the Columbia Hospital complex, which could be converted into student housing and other uses.
