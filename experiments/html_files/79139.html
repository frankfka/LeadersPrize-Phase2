Holloway fires benefits chief

Rick Wood

Acting Milwaukee County Executive Lee Holloway calls for volunteers to read portions of the county’s mission statement and guiding principles Thursday at a pep rally. "We were beat down, made to not feel right, made to feel inferior" by Scott Walker, Holloway told employees.

Milwaukee County exec also holds pep rally to boost employee morale

  • e-mail
  • print
By Steve Schultze of the Journal Sentinel

Jan. 13, 2011 |(190) Comments

Related Coverage
  • Holloway fires benefits chief
  • Daniel Bice: Holloway's guard arrested in tenant spat

Acting County Executive Lee Holloway said Thursday that he fired Milwaukee County's employee benefits manager, the second top administrator to be dumped during Holloway's brief stint as executive.

Holloway declined to give a reason for the dismissal of David Arena and said he was sorry he had to go.

"I tried to save him," Holloway said following a pep rally the executive held as a way to motivate county workers.

Holloway fired county budget director Steve Kreklow on Dec. 28, Holloway's first full day in the county's top job. Holloway, as the County Board chairman, automatically succeeded Scott Walker as county executive after Walker quit to become governor.

Holloway led the effort to create the employee benefits division, and Arena was the first to fill the newly created benefits manager post in 2007. Holloway said Arena shared credit in implementing health insurance contract changes, which are on track to save the county $100 million.

Holloway said the firing had been recommended by Renee Booker, whom Holloway picked for the county's top staff job two weeks ago.

Arena said he was fired Tuesday and was not given a full explanation. He declined to comment on any specific reason he may have been given.

"There's nothing that I know of specifically that I did or had happened that would warrant my removal," said Arena, who was paid about $115,000 a year. He said the job was difficult "because you have a lot of masters. It's almost impossible to keep everybody happy with whatever you do."

Holloway said Gerry Schroeder, the county retirement system manager, had been temporarily assigned Arena's duties.

Supervisor Patricia Jursik said she was disappointed to learn of Arena's firing and that he wouldn't be able to advise the county long-range planning committee that she heads.

"I was not a happy camper today," Jursik said. "We were left without his input."

She said she also was concerned about the series of major personnel and policy decisions Holloway is making during his brief stint as acting executive. Holloway must name an interim county executive by Jan. 27.

He also is a candidate in the Feb. 15 primary to fill the remaining year of Walker's county executive term. The other candidates on the ballot are former state Sen. Jim Sullivan (D-Wauwatosa), state Rep. Jeff Stone (R-Greendale), activist Ieshuh Griffin and philanthropist Chris Abele. The runoff election for the office is April 5.

Thursday's rally was attended by more than 200 county managers and nonunion workers in the third floor courthouse hallway just outside the county executive's office.

Holloway sought to fire up the crowd, urging them to cheer a litany of county "guiding principles." The employees needed the morale boost after eight years of criticism by Walker, Holloway said.

"We were beat down, made to not feel right, made to feel inferior" by Walker, Holloway told employees. "I will not let anyone else come here and mistreat you like you were for the last eight years."

Walker had frequently criticized county employee costs as too high and pushed to privatize several groups of workers.

Holloway sent a letter to all employees Monday praising their work and seeking their ideas on improving county programs.

"Don't be surprised if we send up a few trial balloons as we identify ways to improve the county," Holloway wrote.
