‘Culture of corruption' leads to calls for ethics reform

  • Print
  • E-mail

By Aaron Gould Sheinin

The Atlanta Journal-Constitution

A diverse group of public interest groups on Thursday said Georgia's government has allowed a culture of "low-level conflict of interest and low-level corruption" to grow and that changes are needed.

The Georgia Alliance for Ethics Reform outlined a plan that would cap gifts from lobbyists to lawmakers, give more power to state ethics watchdogs and limit campaign contributions to and from special interest groups. The new group also said ethics reforms passed in 2010 are not strong enough.

"The Georgia Legislature has moved far too slowly and far too little on ethics legislation for years," said Bob Irvin, past president of Common Cause Georgia and a former Republican lawmaker who served as minority leader.

Common Cause has teamed up with Georgia Watch, a consumer watchdog group, the Georgia Tea Party Patriots and gubernatorial candidate-turned-government watchdog Ray Boyd to form the alliance. Irvin said they have had meetings with lawmakers and have received positive feedback, although he said they do not yet have a lawmaker to sponsor the legislation.

The inclusion of the tea party could provide the organization its greatest asset, as the grassroots-based group proved in the 2010 elections to be a major force in Republican politics, Irvin said. Debbie Dooley, a state coordinator for the Georgia Tea Party Patriots, said the group will use what it learned in last year's elections to push ethics reform.

"This is the people's legislation," she said. "This is something people want. We're going to activate the local tea parties in different areas of the state, have them contact their state legislators and ask them to vote for this. This is a priority for us."

But even the tea party might not be enough to get lawmakers to act, said Brian Rell, a former state GOP executive director and campaign strategist.

"With the budget, transportation and then redistricting, there's not enough political oxygen in the room for ethics legislation to pass or receive serious consideration," Rell said.

That's not the issue, though, said Rep. Joe Wilkinson, R-Atlanta, chairman of the House Ethics Committee. New rules lawmakers adopted last year just took effect Jan. 10.

"Why not let us see how it works?" Wilkinson said Thursday. "It's only been in for 10 days."

Speaker David Ralston, R-Blue Ridge, feels the same way, his spokesman Marshall Guest said.

"The speaker sponsored an ethics reform bill last year," he said. "It will be prudent to let this law work before we revisit ethics."

Those new rules require lobbyists to report gifts to lawmakers every two weeks during the session, and they increase lobbyist registration fees and fines for anyone violating the law.

Wilkinson noted that a national organization has ranked Georgia as having one of the toughest ethics laws in the nation.

But Irvin and the alliance pre-emptively dismissed attempts to lean on the recently enacted ethics rules as a reason not to act this year.

"While the law passed last year, we believe, contains some good elements, it was far from being anywhere near strong enough," Irvin said. "When somebody says we ought to give it a chance, that is often a cloak for saying we don't want to do anything."

As for Rell's concern that lawmakers will be too occupied with other issues, Irvin was likewise dismissive.

"If the General Assembly does not pass ethics legislation this year, it's because they don't want to, not because they don't have time," he said.

Ethics proposal

Among highlights of the Georgia Alliance for Ethics Reform's proposal:

  • Limit to $100 any gift from a lobbyist to a public official, their immediate family or staff. No cap currently exists.
  • Require lobbyist gifts to be disclosed within five days. Lobbyists must now disclose spending every two weeks while lawmakers are in session.
  • Give more power to what was formerly known as the State Ethics Commission.
  • Cap transfers from a candidate's campaign account to other candidates, parties, committees or political action committees to $10,000. Transfers to candidates are limited by campaign contribution limits, but there is no cap on transfers to PACs.
  • Limit contributions to PACs and parties to $1,000. There is currently no cap.
