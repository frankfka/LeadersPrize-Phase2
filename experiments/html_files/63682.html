Betty Sutton and Jim Renacci fight confusion and each other in Ohio congressional race (photo gallery)

Posted on August 18, 2012 at 8:00 PM

By Sabrina Eaton, cleveland.com

seaton@cleveland.com

403 Forbidden

403 Forbidden

Everybody seems to know where Jim Renacci and Betty Sutton stand on the issues. Its where their new congressional district stands that has people confused.

Sutton, a three-term Democratic member of Congress from Copley Township, is as reliable a vote for her partys positions as freshman Wadsworth Republican Rep. Jim Renacci is for the GOP.

The polar opposites were put into the same district last year when Ohios Republican-controlled state legislature remapped the states congressional districts, eliminating two seats because Ohios population growth has lagged behind other parts of the country.

Sutton, 49, and Renacci, 53, are facing off in one of the nations two November matchups between congressional incumbents. (The other one is in Iowa.) Its universally rated among the countrys most competitive races. It includes about half of Renaccis old territory and about 20 percent of Suttons.

The nonpartisan Cook Political Report classifies the contest as a toss-up even though the new district was drawn to lean Republican. Polls released by Suttons campaign have shown her with a slight lead thats within the pollsters margin of error.

The newly configured 16th District encompasses all of heavily Republican Wayne County as well as some of western Cuyahoga Countys most reliably conservative areas, such as Strongsville, North Royalton and Westlake. It also contains parts of Summit, Portage, Medina and Stark counties.

Renacci is a businessman and former mayor of Wadsworth who ran for Congress after his Chevrolet dealership was shuttered during General Motors government-orchestrated bankruptcy. Since he defeated former Alliance Democratic Rep. John Boccieri in 2010, Renacci has been a steadfast supporter of efforts to repeal the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act and of other GOP priorities. In an examination of 2011 votes, National Journal magazine ranked Renacci the 87th-most-conservative member of the 435-member House of Representatives.

Sutton is a labor lawyer and former state legislator elected in 2006 to a congressional seat vacated by Sherrod Browns successful Senate run. Legislation she authored gave extra money to members of the armed forces who were kept in the military past their enlistment date and established the temporary Cash for Clunkers program that stimulated auto sales during the recession. National Journal says shes the 89th-most-liberal member of Congress.

While Renacci and Sutton scramble to publicize themselves and their political views in areas theyve never represented, they face bewildered constituents who dont know which congressional district they live in.

As Sutton shook hands with George and Donna OBrien of Berea last week at the Cuyahoga County Fairs main admissions gate, George OBrien told her that he thinks theyre in her new district, but that he isnt sure.

It is hard to know exactly where the people fall by way of their district, but I am going to fight for you anyway, Sutton told the OBriens, who expressed support for a redistricting reform plan that will be on Ohios November ballot. It is crazy trying to figure out who is in what district. It is not fair to the voters. People dont know who their congressperson is.

Sutton approached every police officer she saw at the fair to say Thank you for your service and accosted Cindy Towner of North Olmsted outside a kettle corn booth.

Id appreciate your consideration and hopefully your support, she told Towner.

Towner told Sutton shes familiar with her record and plans to vote for her.

From what Im hearing, I dont care for Renaccis views, said Towner, adding that she disapproves of Republican efforts to cut federal money for Planned Parenthood and fool with Medicare coverage.

At a Cuyahoga County GOP picnic a few days later in Olmsted Falls, Renacci echoed Suttons frustration with the new congressional districts as he walked up and down the line of Republicans awaiting their lunch of hamburgers and hot dogs, shaking hands and introducing himself in an area he has never represented.

If I had anything to do with redistricting, I would have left the districts the way they were and just added a little bit, he said. I am dealt the game that I have, so I have got to play it.

Andy Szcezesniak of North Royalton said he welcomes Renaccis representation in Cuyahoga County. He said he has heard radio interviews with Renacci and has been impressed by his knowledge and business acumen.

We need more people who know how to create jobs and keep jobs going, said Szcezesniak.

Maria Socion of Cleveland buttonholed Renacci with an invitation to speak at Parmas Philippine Cultural Civic Center.

You need to show your face, Sucion told him. People dont know you yet.

I am a firm believer that when they get to know me and my opponent, most of the new district will vote for me, Renacci said, accepting her invitation.

Renacci says he has tried to meet every mayor and council person, Republican or Democrat, in the new district, making as many as five appearances a day. He said he plans to skip most of this months GOP national convention to campaign.

Jim has great grass-roots support in Cuyahoga County, county GOP Chairman Rob Frost said at the picnic. There is a big contrast in this race. It isnt like there are shades of gray.

Sutton, too, is maintaining a hectic schedule getting to know the areas that are new to her and says shell probably skip the entire Democratic National Convention. Stark County Democratic Party Chairman Randy Gonzalez says she has made frequent forays to his area, which was not previously in her district. Gonzalez said that at a recent festival in Jackson Township, Sutton shook hands with hundreds of people despite the 95-degree heat.

Gonzalez says Stark County, formerly in a single district, is now split into three, which has puzzled voters. Sutton and groups that favor her have tried to cut through the confusion by sending direct mail to voters in the countys 16th District section.

When it comes to the campaign dollars the candidates can use to get out their message, Federal Election Commission records show Renacci with an edge. The candidates most recent campaign finance reports show his campaign had $1.5 million in the bank at the end of June, compared with around $900,000 for Sutton. In an effort to close the gap, Sutton held a fundraiser last week with House Democratic Leader Nancy Pelosi.

Sutton says shes confident shell have the resources needed to communicate with the people of the district.

Elections are not just about money, they are about people, she says.

I have never lost an election. Given a choice between me and Renacci, voters will pick someone who will stand up for them.

Here are how a variety of interest groups rate the legislative records of Reps. Jim Renacci and Betty Sutton:

National Rifle Association: Renacci: A, Sutton: F

AFL-CIO: Renacci: 3%, Sutton: 100%

U.S. Chamber of Commerce: Renacci: 100%, Sutton: 35%

NAACP: Renacci: 20%, Sutton: 100%

American Conservative Union: Renacci: 80%, Sutton: 4%

Humane Society of the United States: Renacci: 0%, Sutton: 100%

National Right to Life Committee: Renacci: 100%, Sutton: 0%

National Taxpayers Union: Renacci: 74%, Sutton: 12%

National Education Association: Renacci: F, Sutton: A

League of Conservation Voters: Renacci: 6%, Sutton: 91%

To see an interactive map of Ohio's new congressional districts, click here. A zoom tool can be used to see detailed district borders down to the street level.

Baldwin Wallace University political science department head Thomas Sutton, who is not related to Betty Sutton, says negatives attached to both candidates have put the district into play despite a playing field thats tilted toward Renacci.

He said Renacci has been hurt by an FBI investigation into roughly $100,000 in campaign donations that he accepted from employees of Suarez Corp. and eventually returned, while Sutton has something of a hard edge to her campaign style that puts people off. A study released this year found that Suttons office had the highest staff turnover rate in the U.S. House of Representatives.

The political scientist believes that confusion over the new district lines could hurt Sutton if Democrats in Cuyahoga County who think theyre still in a safely Democratic district dont turn out to back her.

He predicts the contest between Sutton and Renacci could be strongly affected by whichever national candidate is more successful at getting his sympathizers to the polls. President Barack Obamas backers will vote for Sutton, while challenger Mitt Romneys supporters will pick Renacci.

Indeed, Sutton and Renacci both are trying to score points by linking each other to politically controversial actions by the other partys presidential and vice presidential candidates. And theyre not afraid to leave accuracy by the wayside as they do so.

On Wednesday afternoon, Renaccis campaign put out a fundraising solicitation that accused Obama and Sutton of taking $741 billion from seniors to fund Obamacare and contending that Sutton voted to end Medicare as we know it.

The health care reform law actually did not cut money from the Medicare program. It made changes to reduce Medicares future growth, such as reducing payments to private insurers for Medicare Advantage plans, and paying less to hospitals that have frequent readmissions.

Three minutes after Renaccis missive, Suttons campaign put out a news release that asked whether Renacci would stand with Paul Ryan and his anti-senior agenda by appearing with the vice presidential candidate at a North Canton campaign appearance. Her release contended that Ryans budget ends Medicare as we know it.

In fact, Ryans budget makes many of the same reductions to the growth of Medicare that were contained in the health care law that Republicans criticize. It also calls for leaving Medicare intact for people 55 or older but privatizing it for future beneficiaries and giving them government subsidies to buy insurance.

Renacci did appear with Ryan at the Walsh University event and is comfortable enough with the GOP candidates for president and vice president that he tells voters to remember the three Rs: Romney, Ryan and Renacci.

Baldwin Wallaces Sutton, the political scientist, says the 16th District race is all about mobilizing partisans and sharpening ideological differences.

The middle is gone, he says.
